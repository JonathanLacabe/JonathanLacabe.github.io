I. Introduction/General Terms.


# Astronomy: The study of the universe, and the processes by which the objects within it interact with eachother.


# Lightspeed: The distance which light travels within one year, e.g. 9.46 × 10¹² kilometers. Light travels through the Universe at this constant speed: The light from stars is travelling at lightspeed, so if a star is 20 lightyears away from Earth, a viewer on Earth will only see the stars as they looked 20 years previously. Therefore, information can be discerned about what happened billions of years ago, at the dawn of the Universe, by looking at stars farther away.

Light travelling from the sun takes over 8 minutes to the Earth.


# Astronomical Unit: The average distance between the Sun and the Earth, defined as 149,597,870,700 meters.


# A. Rule 1. There are eight planets that orbit around the Sun, along with many other bodies like moons, dwarf planets, and others. These compose the 'Solar System'. Planets are celestial bodies of significant size that orbit a star. If the planet consistently produces its own light, it is a star. Though there is man-made light on Earth, anything involving life doesn't count.

The celestial bodies, including several dwarf planets, drawn to scale but not accurate orbit-wise. Courtesy of Qld Science Teachers.

The Sun is the local star of the solar system (the 'primary' of the Earth), and stars themselves are basically just enormous balls of glowing gas which generate energy through internal nuclear reactions.


# A. Rule 2. All of the stars visible to the naked eye in the night sky are part of the Mily Way Galaxy - there are hundreds of billions of stars in this galaxy, and hundreds of billions of galaxies in the Universe.

The space between the stars is not completely empty: there is a sparse distribution of gas intermixed with tiny particles called 'interstellar dust'. Progressively, the gas and dust collect into huge clouds in each galaxy, which form the raw material for future generations of stars. This interstellar dust is extremely sparse, however, and can thus still serve as a great vacuum. Built-up dust is akin to space smog, blocking the view of more distant regions of the galaxy to Earth.


# A. Rule 3. Because of experimental/observational evidence indicating that there is some unknown material exerting gravitational force upon objects in space, it is believed there is some "Dark Matter" that cannot be directly observed, interfering with everything and frustrating the current 'laws' of Physics.


# A. Rule 4. While many solar systems only have one star (like ours), many systems are double or triple systems, with 2, 3, or more stars revolving around each other. Several places in the galaxy have so many stars they are known as star clusters.


# A. Rule 5. All stars die after running out of "fuel", because stars can only produce energy while they have some fuel source. The Sun, for example, uses nuclear fusion to create fuel, converting hydrogen into helium, which releases energy in the form of light and heat. When stars die, they explode and their star dust is fed back into the Universe for reuse.


# Constellation: The groupings of stars in the night sky that different cultures assigned different meanings - patterns, such as Orion the Hunter and the Big Dipper, are among these groupings.

Orion the Hunter and the constellation that is said to represent him. The bright orange star on his shoulder is called Betelgeuse.

While there are 88 patches of sky which humans have deliminated (additionally serving to simplify observations), there are many less bright night-sky objects that are not included in the constellations.

Many galaxies/celestial objects are named after the constellation in which they reside - for example, the Saggitarius Dwarf Galaxy is in the direction of the Saggitarius constellation.


# A. Rule 6. The Milky Way Galaxy has many orbiting satellite galaxies that will eventually be incorporated/subsumed by the Milky Way Galaxy itself, such as the Magellanic Clouds and the Sagittarius Dwarf Galaxy.

The nearest non-satellite galaxy to the Milky Way is the Andromeda Galaxy (of the Andromeda Constellation), and these two galaxies, along with 50 others nearby, are collectively known as the Local Group, the 'local' galaxy cluster. Most galaxies are found in clusters.


# A. Rule 7. Some galaxy clusters themselves form into larger groups, known as superclusters. The Local Group is part of the Virgo Supercluster, 110 million lightyears across.

Farther away, there are quasars, the especially brilliant centers of galaxies which glow from an 'extremely energetic process'. Normally, at the distance where one could see quasars, galaxies are too dim to see, but because quasars have an energy produced by a gas heated to a temperature of millions of degrees (produced as it gravitates toward a massive black hole and swirls around it), quasars are bright enough to be the most distant objects that can be seen in the expanses of space.

The quasars are 10 billion plus light-years away, and thus show 10 billion years into the past. Beyond these quasars, astronomers detected the feeble afterglow of the Big Bang, detected from all directions in space.


# A. Rule 8. While the Universe is very large, it is also very sparse. In the interstellar gas of the galaxy, there is on average one atom per cubic centimeter, while in intergalactic space (much sparser than the Milky Way galaxy), that number drops to one atom per cubic meter of space.


# A. Rule 9. Summary of the Atomic Level of Matter:

The smallest part of any matter that retains its chemical properties (anything more complex than mono-atomic structures) are the molecules. Water, for example, has the molecule H2O, three atoms bonded together. Molecules are always built of atoms, the smallest division of an element that can be identified as that element.

There are over 100 elements that occur in nature, but most are rare, and only a few occur with any real frequency, known as the “Cosmically Abundant” elements. Listed in descending frequency, they are as follows:
  1. Hydrogen
  2. Helium
  3. Carbon
  4. Nitrogen
  5. Oxygen
  6. Neon
  7. Magnesium
  8. Silicon
  9. Sulfur
  10. Iron
Atoms consist of a central, positively charged nucleus surrounded by negatively charged electrons. The densest part of the atom is in the nucleus (composed of positive protons and neutral neutrons) while the majority of the mass is in the electron cloud around the nucleus.

Elements are defined by the number of protons in its atoms. The distance between the nucleus to the electrons is 100K times that of the diameter of the nucleus itself. Thus, solid matter is mostly empty space, and atoms are much emptier than the solar system, if they were to be placed on the same scale.


# Zenith: The point in the 'space dome' directly above you, from your perspective.


# Horizon: The point where the space dome meets the Earth. In a flat land, the horizon would form a 360° circle around the observer.


# Celestial Sphere: The ancient Greek view of the world as a sphere, of which the outermost shell had stars embedded like sky decorations.


# Axis: The central line of the Earth, going from the North Pole, through the Earth, to the South Pole. The Earth rotates on its axis counterclockwise, from the perspective of the Northern direction.


# Celestial Pole: If the Earth's axis were to be imagined as extended into space, it would be found that there now is a north and south celestial pole in the space dome that the stars rotate around, seen from (almost) any point on Earth:
The trails of the stars over many nights as a result of the rotation of the Earth around the celestial pole.


# Celestial Equator: As an extension of the celestial poles, the Earth's equator can be imagined as stretching off into space in a circular fashion, bisecting the Universe.


# A. Rule 10. Way back when, everyone thought the Universe revolved around the Earth, known as the Geocentric Model of the Universe. This is ignorant and wrong.

This idea was developed for more reasons than sheer human-centrism, however: the sun was about 1° to the east each day, relative to the stars. It takes 1 year for the sun to make a complete circle. This path is elliptic - the Sun rises 4 minutes later each day with respect to the stars. During the day, sunlight is scattered by the molecules of Earth's atmosphere, filling the sky with light and hiding the stars above the horizon.

The elliptic is not along the celestial equator (see definition above), but is rather inclined to it at an angle of 23.5°, because Earth's axis of rotation is tilted by 23.5°. Other planets are so tilted that they orbit the Sun on their side. This tilt is why the Sun moves northward and southward in the sky as the seasons change.


# A. Rule 11. When north of the equator, the 'north celestial pole' will appear above the northern horizon at an angle equal to one's latitude - In San Francisco, 38°N, the north celestial pole is 38° above the northern horizon. The 'south celestial pole' is thus 38° below the southern horizon, and furthermore at 38° South the southern celestial pole will be 38° above the horizon.

The part of the sky however many degrees from the pole (in this case, 38°) will seem to never set, known as the circumpolar zone. There is a particular star right at the Earth's north celestial pole that seems to move minimally during the daily rotations of the heavens: this is Polaris, the North Star.


# A. Rule 12. Apart from the sun, the other planets change their positions slightly everyday. They have their own rotational patterns, 'rising' and 'setting' (in relation to the visibility of the Sun from a particular point on the object) over specific periods of time. The Greeks differentiated these bodies from the rest of the "fixed stars" by calling them planets, or wanderers.

The moon has the fastest planetary motion, since it moves 12° per day. All of the planets have their paths close to the elliptic path of the sun, because the paths of the planets around the sun are all almost in exactly the same plane, like circles on a paper, along with the Earth. Each of the major celestial bodies are found with an 18°-wide belt, centered on the Sun's elliptic path, known as the zodiac. The motions of the planets, as seen from Earth, are somewhat obfuscated by the movement of Earth, making the process rather complex to understand from the Earth as the reference point.


# Asterism: A star pattern within a constellation (or spanning multiple constellations), such as the big Dipper as a part of Ursa Major. Think of them like sub-constellations.


II. Ancient Astronomy.


# Cosmology: The Science of the Cosmos, the human conceptualization and understanding thereof. The Mayans and Ancient Britons used calendar systems to record astronomical observations, such as the Mayan calendar and Stonehenge. Numerous cultures (like the Egyptians and Chinese) developed 365 day calendars, needed for things like the timing of the flooding of the Nile River (which follows a yearly pattern).


# A. Rule 13. Aristotle is the earliest significant scholar in Astronomy, working from 384-322 B.C. - he wrote of how the Moon's phases are caused by differing portions being lit by the sun as the months go by. He knew the Sun was farther away due to the solar eclipses, and that the Earth was spherical due to the shadow on the moon never being indicative of a disk (which, at least during some points of the movement, would be a straight line across).

He, and everyone else at the time, were also convinced that the Universe was revolving around the Earth, because the closer objects in the sky (the 'fixed stars') seem to move on a still distant backdrop, compared to the wandering stars.

This apparent fixed-ness is the result of the Parallax, the shift in direction of an object due to the perspective and motion of the observer. The perceived shift in direction of stars due to the Earth's orbital motion is called stellar parallax, and because it is practically imperceptible in more distance objects (believed to be closer due to their brightness), only two possibilities were immediately clear to the Greeks:

1. The Earth is not moving.
2. The stars are so far away that the Parallax shift is extremely small, much like how far-away mountains do not seem to move when you are moving far away from them.

Of course, they went with the former.


# A. Rule 14. 'Flat Earth Theory' is an ignoramus ideology for the following reasons:

Firstly, if you were to view a ship sailing across the horizon, you would find that it does not shrink and get smaller. The curvature of the Earth will interfere with your view of the ship, and the hull of the ship will disappear over the horizon before the mast does.

Secondly, if the Earth were to be assumed to be a disk, it would not be possible for it to be simultaneously night and day on different parts of the planet. The nature of a disk makes it so when the Sun is facing it, even if the disk is turned a little, it will be lit by the Sun, and thus be day.


# A. Rule 15. Geometric determination of the size of the Earth:

Because the sun is so far away, if everyone on Earth were to point at the Sun while it is visible (the entire enlightened side of the Earth), each arm would be parallel to one another:

The more distant an object, the closer to parallel the rays of light coming from it.

Eratosthenes found that the bottom of a well in Syrene, Egypt, would be perfectly enlightened by the sun at noon on the first day of Summer. At noon of the first of Summer in Alexandria, he found that the sun was not directly overhead, but in fact off by a few degrees (based on the shadow of a column). Therefore, he deduced that in Alexandria the sun was roughly 7° off what it was in Syrene. This being 1/50th of the 360° of the assumed sphere, he multiplied the distance from Syrene to Alexandria by 50 and found a rough estimate of the circumference fo the Earth.


# A. Rule 16. Hipparchus was even more hardcore in his devotion to Astronomical truth than Eratosthenes. He catalogued the sky into a system based on coordinates and brightness - the position of objects in the sky were catalogued in a primitive celestial latitude and longitude system, and he divided the sky into different apparent magnitudes according to their brightness. The brightest stars were of the first magnitude, for example.

He then made a horrifying discovery.

The position of the sky of the north celestial pole, as it had appeared in the centuries prior and then, had shifted in position somewhat. The rate with which the entire sky dome rotates is slow, continuous, and constant. The North Celestial Pole is just the projection of Earth's North Pole into the sky - if the north celestial pole is wobbling around, then it is the Earth that is doing the wobbling. # A. Rule 17. The last ancient Astronomer who wrote hugely consequential astronomical treatises was Ptolemy, who wrote a compendium of astronomical knowledge known as the Almagest in 140-ish A.D.. This book also discusses many works from the past, saving knowlege of those works for future generations.

Ptolemy benefited from the mass of astronomical knowledge hoarded over the prior centuries, especially that of Hipparchus, and produced the Ptolemaic Model of the Universe, which no one expanded upon for 1000+ years. See Rule 18 for a full description.

III. Astrology.


# A. Rule 19. While Astrology is not bound to reality, it is interesting to learn about it from a historical perspective. The seven classical wandering stars, including the sun and the moon, hold a special place in the mythos of different cultures as they developed and formed their own belief systems.

As a means of explaining the inexplicable Universe (and to support the notions that humans are central to it), weather, solar exlipses, and the patterns of birds were all assigned divine significance. Applied to Astronomy, "Astrology" was born.

The Babylonians used the knowledge of the stars to guide rulers in decision-making. By the 2nd century B.C., the Greeks applied Astrology to every individual - "Natal Astronomy" was formed through the belief that the configuration of the stars at the moment of one's birth will have an effect on their life and success. Ptolemy took this belief system to the next level by writing the "Tetrabiblos", the treatise on Astrology that transformed natal astronomy into something akin to a religion.


# A. Rule 20. The Horoscope is the central part of natal astronomy, the identifying of the time of birth with the relationship to the movement of the stars that supposedly determines one's personality traits.

If religion is the opium of the masses, Astrology is the opium of the ignoramuses. The zodiac, as mentioned in Rule 12, is divided into 12 signs, sections, each 30° long as part of the 360° celestial sphere. Each sign is named after a constellation in that section of the sky where the sun, moon, and planets were seen to pass.


# A. Rule 21. An individual's "sign" is their sun sign, e.g. the part of the zodiac the sun was in when they were born. However, because of precession (see Rule 15), the sky has been rotating over time, and in the 2000 years since the Tetrabiblos was written, the stars have shifted 1/12 the zodiac - about 1/2 of a sign. To stop themselves from allowing reason to infiltrate their minds, practioners of Astrology retain the position of stars as they were in the time of Ptolemy as their "sun sign", which makes even less sense than having the dates of the signs change over time. This speaks to the meaningless and pseudoscientific nature of Astrology as a whole, which, as an ancient spiritualistic practice, has had a remarkable lifespan past the Age of Enlightenment.


IV. Origin of Modern Astronomy.


# Modern Astronomy:
While Europe was going througn an anti-intellectual phase during the Middle Ages, there was a flowering of Astronomical discovery and progress during the Islamic Golden Age, which preserved and elaborated on the ideas of the Greeks. It was the end of the Dark Ages in Europe and the ushering in of the Renaissance that saw a revival in intellectual interest in sceintific, and importantly Astronomical, ideas in Europe. Copernicus embodied the spirit of European revival in interest in Astronomy (see below).


# A. Rule 22. Nicolaus Copernicus was a Polish astronomer who flourished during the early 1500s. He led the critical reappraisal of Ptolemy's ancient model of the Universe, which had stood as the dominant explanation of the cosmos for over a thousand years. He led the formation of the Heliocentric model of the solar system, which radically considered Earth itself to be a planet that orbited the sun, alongside all other planets. Additionally, he theorized that only the moon orbits the Earth.
Copernicus developed a heliocentric plan of the solar system. This system was published in the first edition of De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium. Notice the word Sol for “Sun” in the middle.

As evident, he still assumed that the orbits of the planets would be uniformally circular. However, his ideas were revolutionary enough to provoke discussion across the scientific world, and the tenets of heliocentrism would eventually be popularly accepted over a century after his death. Controversially (as with everything he theorized), he explained the precession of the celestial sphere as being the result of the rotation of the Earth along its axis.


# A. Rule 23. Copernicus's masterpiece, De Revolutionibus, elaborates on Earth as one of six planets, and correctly lists the Planets in order of their proximity to the sun, and was able to deduce that the closer to the sun the planet, the greater the orbital speed.

Furthermore, complexities like retrograde motion were explained through this new theory with much simpler explanations than Ptolemy. This theory conflicted with the thousands of years of established common sense: the greek/classical schools of thought, and the dogma pushed by the Catholic Church.


# A. Rule 24. The scientific method, nonexistant during the time of Copernicus, was developed by Galileo Galileo during the 17th century. He greatly contributed to the understandings of Mechanics, Classical Physics of motion, and the influence of forces on bodies. Galileo theorized the basic principle of Inertia, that rest is no more natural a state for a body than motion. He argued that a force is required not only to start an object moving from rest, but also for slowing down, stopping, speeding up, and changing direction in any form. Additionally, in studying acceleration (see P. Rule 12), he found that objects will accelerate uniformally as they free fall or are otherwise influenced by gravity. These laws would be formulated in exact mathematical expressions.

Most importantly, he adopted the heliocentric model of the universe in the 1590s and began lecturing on the topic. The church pushed back, decreeing that heliocentrism was "false and absurd" in 1616 and disavowing its defense.

Galileo would drastically improve upon the telescope and its powers of magnification. He used this invention to observe the cosmos, beginning in 1609, and he discovered many distant stars too faint to be seen with the naked eye, and that the Milky Way across the night sky was made out of many stars as well.

He found that Jupiter had its own moons, circling Jupiter at different orbit speeds, proving that centers of motion could themselves be in motion (a theory argued against by geocentrics). Venus proved to go through phases like the moon, showing it revolved around the sun.

For these discoveries, Galileo was rewarded with a house arrest on the orders of the Catholic Church, who found his work heretical, and this would last until his death. In this confinement he would write one of his greatest masterpieces, the "Two New Sciences" which would form the foundations for modern Physics.


# A. Rule 25. Tycho Brahe was a Danish astronomer who worked concurrent to Galileo, and produced a sound mathematical basis (along with Kepler) of Copernicus's Theory of Heliocentrism.

He established an observatory on a North Sea Island, and he is said to be the last of the pre-telescopic observers in Europe. He took extremely detailed records of the positions of the Sun, Moon, and Planets for almost 20 years, the greatest collection of such data since Hipparchus.

When he fled Denmark because of all the enemies he'd accumulated, he became the court Astronomer in Prague, where he enlisted the help of the young Astronomer Johannes Kepler in analyzing the data (see below).


# A. Rule 26. Johannes Kepler was a German astronomer who served as Tycho Brahe's assistant in Prague. It was only after Brahe's death did Kepler gain access to the entirety of the records, which occupied him for the next 20 years. In this analysis, he developed three laws that dictate the motion of planets through Space, known as Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion.

Law of Planetary Motion #1 - The Orbit of a planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci.

Explanation: The path of an object through space is called its orbit. Kepler assumed, at first, that the orbits of planets were circles, but doing so only produced orbits that were consistent with Brahe's observations. He discovered that the orbit of Mars was elliptical rather than circular.

All of these shapes are closed curves, belonging to a family of curves known as conic sections.
The circle, ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola are all formed by the intersection of a plane with a cone. This is why such curves are called conic sections.


The center of a circle, of course, is a special point: the distance from the center of a cicle to anywhere on the circle is always the same - that is the radius. In an ellipse there are TWO special points, known as the foci, or the focus points of the ellipse. The sum of the distance from the focus points to any position on the ellipse is always the same:
An animated ellipse to illustrate that the sum of the distances from a point to the foci is constant. Courtesy of the UTSA.


The foci will, of course, change their postion depending on the size/nature of the ellipse. The widest diameter of the ellipse is called its Major Axis, while half that distance, the distance from the center of the ellipse from end to end, is called the Semimajor Axis. The smallest diameter of the ellipse is the Minor Axis (of Symmetry), perpendicular to the Major Axis, and it has two semimajor axes at either side of the center as well:
The Major Axis – the longest diameter of an ellipse, each end point is called a vertex.
The Minor Axis – the shortest diameter of an ellipse, each end point is called a co-vertex.
The Semimajor Axis (a) – Half of the major axis.
The Semiminor Axis (b) – Half of the minor axis.
Eccentricity (e) – the distance between the two focal points, F1 and F2, divided by the length of the major axis.
ae – the distance between one of the focal points and the centre of the ellipse (the length of the Semimajor axis multiplied by the eccentricity). Courtesy of the Science & Math Zone.


The Semimajor axis of the orbit of Mars (also the planet's average distance from the Sun) is 228 million kilometers.

The shape/roundness of an ellipse depends on how close together the two foci are, compared with the Major Axis. The ratio of the distance between the foci to the length of the major axis is called the Eccentricity of the ellipse. If the eccentricity is zero, then the foci will be in the same spot and the ellipse will be a circle. Thus, in elliptical terms, a cricle is an ellipse of zero eccentricity with the Semimajor axis as the radius.

The greater the eccentricity, the more elongated the ellipse, up to a maximum eccentricity of 1.0, which is just a flat line. The size and shape of an ellipse are completely specified by its Semimajor axis and its Eccentricity. Mars has an elliptical orbit of 0.1, with the sun at one of the foci, the other being empty. This discovery was generalized by Kepler to apply to all orbits, with different eccentricities.


# A. Rule 27.
Law of Planetary Motion #2 - A line segment joining a planet and the sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time.

The 2nd Law deals with orbital speed, or the speed with which each planet moves along its ellipse. Kepler determined that Mars moves faster as it comes closer to the Sun, and slows down as it pulls away from the Sun.

Visualize an elastic band connecting a celestial body with the Sun. As the body gets farther from the sun, the band gets stretched, and thus moves slower until the band will pull it back to the sun. When it is closer to the sun, the band is not stretched as much and thus moves rapidly. Additionally, if you were to imagine the area sweeped in the ellipse (centered from the sun) by the orbit, then, given equal intervals of time, any two sweeped areas of the orbit will be equal.
The orbital speed of a planet traveling around the Sun (the circular object inside the ellipse) varies in such a way that in equal intervals of time (t), a line between the Sun and a planet sweeps out equal areas (A and B). Note that the eccentricities of the planets’ orbits in the solar system are substantially less than shown here.

While a circular orbit would cause a planet to move at the same speed throughout its orbit, the differing speeds of the planets as they move make it evident that their orbits are elliptical.


# Kinematics: The Science of motion, such as Newton's & Kepler's Laws.


# A. Rule 28. Law of Planetary Motion #3 - The square of a planet's orbital period is proportional to the cube of the length of the Semimajor axis of its orbit.

While the first two laws deal largely with the shape and speeds of a planet's orbit, there was yet to be a mathematical model for the spacing of the planets and how they ended up in the positions/orbits they have.

It was Kepler's 1619 discovery of a basic relationship relating the orbits of the planets to their relative distance from the sun that a mathematical model governing planetary spacing was formed. The Orbital Period P is the time it takes a planet to travel once around the Sun, and the Semimajor axis of a planet's orbit is equivalent to its average distance from the sun. In mathematical terms, this law can be defined as the following:

a³ ∝ P²
a³ = (M1 + M2) × P²

a = Length of the Semimajor axis of the planet's orbit, measured in AU.
M1 = The mass of the object being orbited.
M2 = The mass of the planet.
P = The planet's orbital period, measured in years.

With regard to the Earth (and all other planets where years and AU are the given units), the sides of the formula are not only proportional, but equal. The Third Law of Planetary Motion applies for all objects orbiting the sun, and enables the determination of an objects relative distance from the Sun based on how long the Orbital Period is.

Since Mars has an orbital period of 1.88 years, the third law tells us that (1.88)² = a³, using years and AU as the units. Therefore, the Semimajor axis of Mars has a length of ∛3.53 AU, or 1.52 AU, thus orbiting farther from the Sun than Earth.


# A. Rule 29. Kepler's laws are highly qualitative, as they only reflect rules of thumb that Kepler discerned about the planetary motion in the solar systems. The laws do not actually explain what forces of nature are causing the celestial body to act in the ways specified; that is what makes them 'Laws' (see video for reference).

It would be Isaac Newton who would form the sound mathematical framework that explained the observations and rules formulated by Galileo, Brahe, Kepler, and the rest. As a professor of Mathematics at Cambridge, Newton dedicated much of his time to pondering the cosmos and reality, and from this he developed special mathematical models/equations and laws to govern motion itself, the forces behind Kinematics. He wrote down his theories in a book called the Principia, in 1687, where he unleashed unto an unsuspecting public his three laws of motion:

Law of Motion #1: Every object will continue to be in a state of rest, or move at a constant speed in a straight line, until it is compelled to change by an outside force. The first law is just a restatement of one of Galileo's discoveries, called the Conservation of Momentum (see P. Section VIII.II). This law states that in the abscence of any outside influence, there is a measure of a body's motion, called its momentum, that remains unchanged.

The first law is also oftentimes called the Law of Inertia, where Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist a change in the state of motion (see the Physics reference). In other words, a stationary object will stay at rest while a moving object will keep moving until some force intervenes.


# A. Rule 30. Momentum relies on three factors:
  1. Speed, how fast an object moves (zero if stationary).

  2. The direction of the motion, whether using relative, cartesian, or cardinal directions.

  3. The mass of the body, a measure of the amount of matter in a body.

The speed (with only magnitude) and direction of motion can be combined as velocity (see Classical Physics Section I for explanations and interpretations of velocity & speed). 20 kilometers per hour due South is in terms of Velocity, while 20 kilometers per hour by itself is in terms of speed.

Atoms consist of protons, neutrons, and electrons. Protons and neutrons are in the nucleus, while electrons orbit the nucleus. All these particles have mass and thus create gravitational forces, but gravity is negligible at the atomic scale due to the small masses involved.

Kirchhoff's Laws of Spectroscopy
When light is emitted in the Universe, it always follows one of three patterns, determined by the density of the emitting material. These patterns are defined by Kirchhoff's Laws:

1. Continuous Spectrum - A dense object, like a solid or liquid, emits a continuous spectrum containing all colors of the rainbow. While all colors are present, one color is usually more prominent, depending on the temperature of the object.

2. Emission-Line Spectrum - A low-density gas emits light at specific, well-defined colors. The rest of the spectrum is dark, with no light emitted at other wavelengths.

3. Absorption-Line Spectrum - When light from a dense object passes through a low-density gas, the continuous spectrum loses specific colors. These missing colors correspond to the wavelengths absorbed by the gas.

Density is the key factor in determining which pattern is observed. Dense objects create continuous spectra (unless the object has an atmosphere, like the Earth, in which case it would produce an absorption line spectrum), while low-density gases produce emission or absorption-line spectra based on the context.

Spectral lines are essential in astronomy, providing more information than images. Emission-line and absorption-line spectra both involve gases and the behavior of electrons in atoms, which can only occupy specific orbits around the nucleus.

Spectral lines are general terms referring to the discrete wavelengths of light that appear in a spectrum due to the interaction of light with matter. These lines can either be bright or dark. Absorption lines occur when atoms, ions, or molecules in a cooler medium absorb specific wavelengths of light from a continuous spectrum passing through them, creating dark lines against the background of the continuous spectrum. They indicate the wavelengths of light absorbed by the material, corresponding to the energy differences between specific quantum states. Conversely, emission lines are bright lines that result from atoms, ions, or molecules emitting light at specific wavelengths when their electrons transition from higher to lower energy states. Emission lines are typically observed in hot, excited gases or plasmas.


Electrons can only exist in fixed orbits, or "shells." Moving between orbits requires specific energy changes. When an electron jumps down to a lower orbit, it emits a photon—a piece of light—with an energy corresponding to the difference between the orbits. This emitted light creates an emission-line spectrum, where the light has specific colors unique to the atom. For example, an electron jumping from orbit 3 to orbit 1 may emit blue light, while a jump from orbit 2 to orbit 1 might emit green light.

An atom that gives off many red emission lines would have many closely spaced orbits. This is because while red is a long wave of light, it is a low amount of energy. An atom with closely spaced orbits means electrons drop and jump only a tiny amount and thus give off or absorb only tiny energy colors of light.

The reverse is also true. If the right color of light (matching the energy difference) hits an atom, it can be absorbed, causing the electron to jump to a higher orbit. Light that doesn’t match these energy gaps passes through unaffected. This selective absorption creates an absorption-line spectrum, where specific colors are missing from a continuous spectrum.

Each element has a unique set of electron orbits and, therefore, a distinct pattern of spectral lines. For instance, hydrogen and helium have completely different emission-line spectra, like fingerprints. This allows astronomers to identify the elements present in a star or galaxy simply by analyzing its light.

The Sun’s spectrum, for example, is a continuous spectrum with absorption lines where elements in its atmosphere have absorbed specific colors. These lines reveal the Sun’s composition and help us understand stars across the galaxy.

Stars produce continuous spectra because they are dense, and their color is determined by temperature. Cooler stars emit redder light, and hotter stars emit bluer light. When observing a star like Sirius, absorption lines are visible in its spectrum. These lines occur as light from the dense core passes through the star's less dense outer layers.

Planets also produce continuous spectra due to their dense cores. Absorption lines appear in their spectra if they have atmospheres. These lines allow astronomers to identify atmospheric components, such as oxygen and water, which are essential for life. Earth’s absorption-line spectrum reveals atmospheric features that influence how energy interacts with the planet.


When an atom is excited, its electron gains extra energy and moves to a higher orbit. This energy is temporary, and the electron will eventually return to the lowest orbit, releasing energy in the form of light (emission lines).

Absorption lines occur when an atom absorbs light and the electron jumps to a higher orbit. The electron is forced into this excited state because it "wants" to stay in the lowest energy state. The right energy (specific light colors) is needed to excite the electron.

In a hydrogen atom, the electron can only absorb certain colors depending on the jump between orbits. The larger the jump between orbits, the more energy is needed. For example, the jump from orbit 1 to orbit 2 in hydrogen requires more energy than jumps between higher orbits. This explains why certain absorption lines, like those in the hydrogen spectrum, are only observable under specific conditions.

The sun and Sirius are hot enough to produce the necessary light to excite hydrogen atoms and show absorption lines. However, cooler stars (like some in the Orion constellation) may not generate enough energy to excite their hydrogen atoms, making those stars invisible through absorption lines, despite containing hydrogen. This is a limitation when using absorption lines to determine the composition of stars.


When a star becomes too hot, its inner layers emit high-energy light. This intense radiation can ionize atoms in the outer layers. Ionization occurs when an electron absorbs enough energy to escape the atom entirely, leaving the atom without any electrons.

For example, a helium atom can lose its electron if it absorbs a high-energy photon, resulting in ionization. Once ionized, the atom can no longer produce spectral lines because there are no electrons left to jump between orbits.

If a star is hot enough, such as a powerful blue star, its hydrogen atoms can be fully ionized. Even though these stars are predominantly made of hydrogen, we would not observe their hydrogen spectral lines because all the hydrogen has been ionized, making the star's hydrogen invisible in terms of absorption lines.


The "Goldilocks problem" refers to the need for a star's temperature to be "just right" to observe spectral lines. If a star is too cold, it can't excite its atoms, so no spectral lines are produced. If it's too hot, the intense energy ionizes the atoms, making them invisible. However, if the star's temperature is just right, it excites its atoms without ionizing them, allowing us to see the spectral lines. This balance is crucial for understanding the temperature of a star using spectral lines.

Each element has its own "Goldilocks temperature," at which it will show specific spectral lines. By analyzing which elements are visible, we can determine the star's temperature more accurately than by simply observing its color. This dual-method approach helps scientists ensure their measurements are correct


Annie Cannon, a Harvard computer, realized that a star's temperature was the key factor in determining which spectral lines were visible. She reorganized the classification system by temperature, discarding the old system (A, B, C, D, E, etc.) and establishing the O B A F G K M sequence that is still used today. O stars are the hottest and bluest, while M stars are the coolest and reddest. Our Sun is a G-type star, with a mid-range temperature.


The luminosity of a star is determined by both its temperature and size. Hotter stars are more luminous because they emit more energy. However, size also plays a significant role; a larger star will be more luminous than a smaller star at the same temperature. Stars are classified into three size categories: Class I (Supergiants), which are the largest, Class III (Giants), and Class V (Main Sequence stars), with the Sun being a typical example of a Class V star. By combining a star's size and temperature, we can determine its luminosity, which provides insights into its history and future.

Early attempts to determine Earth's age relied on historical documents, such as ancient religious texts. These efforts, undertaken by various cultures like the Aztecs, Hindus, and Christians, yielded vastly different results, ranging from approximately 1000 CE to 4 billion BCE. These methods suffered from two key flaws: differing cultural concepts of time (cyclical vs. linear) and the unsupported assumption that document writers were present near the Earth's beginning. These approaches relied on untested assumptions, such as the length of ideal cycles or the duration of generations.

A more scientific approach emerged using radioactive decay. This method leverages the predictable rate at which unstable atoms transform, known as their half-life, which is unaffected by external factors like temperature or density. By measuring the ratio of remaining unstable atoms to their decay products in a solid sample, one can calculate the time elapsed since the sample solidified.

However, this method is complicated by the fact that molten rock resets this "atomic clock," as atoms are free to move until the rock solidifies. Because Earth has experienced widespread volcanic activity, determining its age required a sample that hadn't undergone melting. Therefore, the accepted age of Earth, 4.567 billion years, was determined using extraterrestrial rocks.

A planet's atmosphere is its gaseous envelope, with gases forming the outermost layer due to their low density. Terrestrial planets have notably thin atmospheres. Earth's atmosphere, for instance, extends roughly the distance between the 101 and 118 freeways.

A key function of these atmospheres is the greenhouse effect, where incoming solar energy warms the surface, and outgoing infrared radiation is partially absorbed by atmospheric gases like carbon dioxide (CO2) and water vapor (H2O), trapping heat and further warming the surface. This effect primarily warms the surface, not the planet's interior.

Atmospheres also provide some protection from incoming space rocks (meteors), burning up smaller ones (up to building size). Mars, a small terrestrial planet, exhibits transitional features. While its interior cooled, it remained warm enough for past volcanic activity but never developed tectonic plates. Mercury exemplifies small terrestrial planets. Its interior has cooled completely, halting volcanic activity shortly after its formation, and it lacks tectonic plates. Its surface, therefore, is ancient, retaining craters and dating back to the planet's origin (4.567 billion years).

Asteroids are small, rocky, and metallic objects located primarily in the inner solar system, with most of them concentrated between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter in what is known as the asteroid belt. These remnants of the early solar system are typically a few city blocks in size, and there are around 100,000 asteroids larger than one kilometer. While most remain safely within the belt, a few cross Earth's orbit, posing a potential impact threat, as illustrated by the Chicxulub impact that contributed to the extinction of the dinosaurs.

When an asteroid enters Earth's atmosphere, it compresses the gas in front of it, causing intense heat that often vaporizes the rock. This process creates a glowing streak called a meteor, or "shooting star." If any part of the rock survives and lands on Earth's surface, it is termed a meteorite, which can have significant scientific and monetary value. Although direct fatalities from meteorites are rare, incidents have been recorded, such as a woman in the U.S. who suffered a severe bruise after a meteorite crashed through her roof.

Meteor showers occur when Earth passes through trails of space debris, resulting in numerous meteors visible at once. These trails, composed of space pebbles, are remnants of comets or other celestial objects and reflect Earth's motion through the solar system. The phenomenon highlights the dynamic and interconnected nature of the solar system's smaller objects.


Comets are icy, rocky objects from the outer solar system that periodically visit the inner solar system. When they approach the Sun, the ice on their surface sublimates—transforming directly from a solid into gas due to the lack of atmospheric pressure in space. This process creates a visible tail as the solar wind blows the vaporized material away from the Sun. The sublimation of ice can also eject rocky debris, which forms trails in the comet's path. These debris trails are responsible for meteor showers when planets like Earth pass through them, resembling rain hitting a moving windshield.

Another significant group of outer solar system objects is the Kuiper Belt, a vast region beyond Neptune's orbit containing icy and rocky bodies. Kuiper Belt Objects (KBOs) are similar in composition to comets but remain in the distant reaches of the solar system. Pluto, once classified as a planet, is now recognized as one of many KBOs, reflecting its shared characteristics with other objects in the belt. Studying KBOs allows scientists to compare them with inner solar system asteroids, providing crucial insights into the formation and early history of our solar system.


The formation of the solar system is reconstructed through patterns observed today, as no one was present to witness its creation. Key evidence includes the consistent counter-clockwise orbital and spin direction of most planets, their alignment along the Sun’s equator, and the contrasting compositions of the inner and outer solar system. These patterns suggest a shared origin. The prevailing explanation is the Solar Nebula hypothesis, which posits that the solar system began as a large, rotating cloud of gas and dust primarily composed of hydrogen and helium.

As the Solar Nebula contracted under its own gravity, its rotation increased due to the conservation of angular momentum, similar to how a figure skater spins faster by pulling their arms in. This faster rotation created a centrifugal force that counteracted gravity more strongly along the equator than at the poles. Consequently, the cloud flattened into a disk-like shape, with material concentrated along its equatorial plane. This explains why planets orbit in a disk-like alignment and why their motion adheres to Kepler's Laws. The next step in the solar system's evolution involved the transformation of this disk into planets, guided by processes influenced by factors such as the Ice Line.


Planet formation within the Solar Nebula depends on the presence of solids, as gases are too diffuse to combine and form larger bodies. The process begins with tiny solid dust particles sticking together, gradually growing into larger structures such as sand, pebbles, rocks, boulders, and eventually asteroids and planets. However, the ability of materials to exist as solids depends on temperature, which varies with distance from the Sun.

Closer to the Sun, in the Inner Solar System, high temperatures (hundreds to thousands of degrees) ensure that only metals and rocks remain solid, while water and gases like hydrogen and helium exist in gaseous states. This results in planets like Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars forming primarily from rock and metal. In contrast, the Outer Solar System, where temperatures are below freezing, allows water to solidify and join rock and metal in forming planetary bodies. This distinction creates a boundary known as the Ice Line, separating rocky planets of the Inner Solar System from icy and rocky bodies in the Outer Solar System.

While this explains the formation of ice giants like Uranus and Neptune, the gas giants Jupiter and Saturn require additional explanation. Both began as "seeds" of rock, metal, and ice, but their massive size allowed them to exert significant gravitational pull. Over time, this gravity enabled them to accumulate hydrogen and helium gases, the most abundant materials in the solar nebula. This runaway growth in mass and gravity transformed Jupiter and Saturn from ice giants into predominantly gaseous planets, or gas giants.

Thus, the Solar Nebula's temperature gradient and the Ice Line shaped the diverse planetary composition observed in our solar system.


Other Solar Systems:
Other solar systems form through the same processes as our own, with minor variations in the proportions of rock, metal, ice, hydrogen, and helium. However, one key difference is the extent of "planetary migration," where planets shift their orbits due to the presence of gas in the young solar system. Before the central star fully ignites and blows away the surrounding gas, this gas creates friction, allowing planets to spiral inward or outward. In some cases, jovian planets migrate inward toward their star, becoming "hot Jupiters." These are gas giants located in the warm, inner regions of their systems, a phenomenon initially surprising to astronomers.

The intense heat of these inner regions can cause the atmospheres of hot Jupiters to evaporate, as seen in the example of Osiris, a planet slowly losing its gaseous envelope. Over time, such planets may be stripped down to their rocky or icy cores, creating "super Earths"—massive terrestrial planets that were once gas giants. This migration and transformation process highlights the dynamic nature of planetary systems.

One remarkable example of an extreme exoplanet is CoRoT-7b, a terrestrial planet so close to its star that its surface is an ocean of molten rock. The intense heat even causes some of this liquid rock to vaporize, creating a rock-based atmosphere. This extraordinary phenomenon underscores the diverse and often mind-blowing characteristics of planets in the universe.


The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram relies on accurate measurements of stellar distances, which we can achieve using the concept of parallax. Parallax is the apparent shift in the position of an object when the observer's perspective changes. This effect can be demonstrated by holding a finger in front of your face and alternately closing one eye at a time, observing how the finger appears to move relative to the background. The apparent "movement" is not actual but rather an illusion caused by the change in viewpoint.

In astronomy, parallax is a crucial method for determining distances to stars. By observing a star from Earth at different points in its orbit around the Sun (separated by six months), we create two distinct perspectives. The angular shift of the star relative to the distant background, known as the parallax angle, allows us to calculate its distance. The relationship is inverse: larger angular shifts indicate closer objects, while smaller shifts correspond to more distant objects. For instance, if a star's parallax angle is halved, its distance is doubled; if the angle is one-tenth, the distance is ten times greater. This straightforward method provides a foundation for mapping stellar positions and understanding their properties.

Gravity is a pulling force that acts inward, created by mass, and its strength depends on both the amount of mass and the distance from it. In contrast, pressure is an outward-pushing force that acts against an area. In the context of gases, pressure arises from atoms constantly bouncing around, colliding with surfaces, and exerting force. The strength of this pressure is determined by two factors: temperature and density.

Temperature controls how fast gas atoms move; higher temperatures mean faster-moving atoms, which collide with greater force, resulting in higher pressure. Lower temperatures lead to slower atoms and weaker pressure. Density, on the other hand, dictates the number of atoms in a given area. Higher density means more atoms colliding, increasing pressure, while lower density reduces collisions and pressure. The relationship between these factors is captured in the Ideal Gas Law, where pressure is directly proportional to both density and temperature.

In astronomy, density often drives changes in pressure and temperature. When density increases, pressure and temperature rise, and when density decreases, they fall. This interconnected behavior shapes the dynamics of gases in the universe, and density typically takes precedence in governing the other variables. The interplay between gravity and pressure is a fundamental rivalry in astronomy, influencing the behavior and evolution of stars and other celestial phenomena.


The rivalry between pressure and gravity is fundamental to the existence and behavior of stars. Gravity works to reduce energy by pulling matter inward, concentrating it into smaller volumes. Pressure, driven by temperature and density, opposes gravity by trying to reduce energy through expansion, spreading matter outward. These forces are inherently opposed and will never reconcile, yet their balance is essential for the existence of stars.

If only gravity existed, stars would collapse into singularities. If only pressure existed, stars would expand endlessly and cease to exist as discrete entities. The interplay of pressure and gravity creates equilibrium, enabling stars to form and maintain their structure. When gravity dominates, matter shrinks, while stronger pressure causes expansion. If the two forces are balanced, the star remains stable.

This balance can be observed in various settings, such as Earth's atmosphere. At sea level, the gravitational force of the atmosphere above compresses the air, requiring strong pressure to counteract it. On mountaintops, less atmosphere above means weaker gravitational pull and, consequently, lower pressure. According to the Ideal Gas Law, higher pressure at sea level correlates with denser, warmer air, while lower pressure at high altitudes corresponds to cooler, less dense air. These conditions explain phenomena like the need for warm clothing and oxygen masks at high altitudes.

In stars, this same principle applies. The core, bearing the weight of the entire star, experiences immense gravitational compression and requires intense pressure to counteract it. In contrast, the outer layers of the star face less gravitational pull and need only slight pressure to balance. This constant interplay of pressure and gravity determines the stability and lifecycle of stars, making it a central theme in astrophysics and a critical concept for understanding stellar behavior.


Stars shine because they are hot, and they continuously emit energy due to their luminosity. However, unlike everyday objects that cool down when left alone, stars like the Sun maintain their heat and shine consistently over billions of years. This persistence requires a balance: the energy lost through luminosity must be replaced by a source of heat. This balance is known as Fusion vs. Luminosity.

Initially, early scientists believed stars simply cooled over time, transitioning through spectral types (O to M) as they lost energy. Using the rate equation to calculate the lifespan of a star without an energy source, they concluded that stars like the Sun would only last about 800 years—clearly incorrect, as there is abundant evidence that the Sun has existed far longer. This discrepancy indicated that stars must have a mechanism to replenish the heat lost through luminosity.

The eventual discovery of nuclear fusion provided the answer. Fusion is the process by which a star converts energy stored in its fuel—hydrogen—into heat and light. This conversion allows stars to sustain their temperature and luminosity for extended periods, maintaining a dynamic equilibrium. This realization marked a profound moment in scientific history, highlighting the power of evidence and reasoning in uncovering the underlying processes of nature. Fusion, as the internal engine of stars, ensures their enduring brilliance and stability.


The energy source for stars has been a fundamental question in astrophysics. Early ideas proposed chemical combustion, like a giant lump of coal, which would power the Sun for only 1,000 years. Similarly, gravitational impacts, such as celestial bodies colliding with the Sun, were considered. While this could sustain the Sun for about 1 million years, it was still insufficient given geological evidence of Earth's age. Gravitational contraction, where the Sun shrinks under its gravity to generate heat, was a more refined hypothesis, extending the Sun's lifespan to 30 million years. However, this too fell short, necessitating a deeper explanation.

The breakthrough came with Einstein's theory of relativity, particularly the equation e = mc², which revealed that mass and energy are interchangeable. This understanding paved the way to identifying nuclear fusion as the Sun's energy source. Fusion occurs when hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium, releasing vast amounts of energy due to a slight loss of mass. For instance, four hydrogen atoms fuse into one helium atom, but the resulting helium has slightly less mass than the sum of the hydrogens, with the "missing" mass converted into energy. This process provides enough energy to sustain the Sun for approximately 11 billion years, aligning with geological and astronomical observations.

Fusion, however, does not happen spontaneously. Atomic nuclei repel each other due to their positive charges. Overcoming this repulsion requires extremely high temperatures to provide the necessary kinetic energy to force nuclei together. These conditions exist only in the cores of stars, where temperatures are immense due to gravitational compression. Thus, the core is where energy is generated through fusion, while the surface radiates this energy as luminosity. The energy flows from the core outward through processes like radiation and convection, maintaining the star's balance and sustaining its light and heat.


Energy is transferred in three primary ways: radiation, convection, and conduction. Radiation involves the movement of energy through light, where energy transports itself from one place to another. This process is most effective in low-density environments, like the vacuum of space, where light travels from the Sun to Earth. Convection, on the other hand, involves energy being carried by matter. In this process, hot matter expands, becoming less dense, and rises, transferring energy with it. A common example is boiling water, where hot water rises to the top of the pot, carrying heat with it.

Conduction involves energy being transferred through direct contact, similar to how people in a crowded room pass energy from one to another by bumping into each other. This is the mechanism behind heat transfer when you touch a hot oven, as fast-moving atoms in the oven's metal transfer energy to the atoms in your finger, causing a burn. Conduction is also why double-paned windows are effective; the air between the panes prevents the atoms of the inner and outer glass from transferring energy through contact. These methods—radiation, convection, and conduction—are the fundamental ways through which energy moves within and between stars.


Gas clouds exist in outer space, and their presence is crucial to the formation of stars. These clouds, though difficult to observe directly, are a fundamental part of the cosmic "ecosystem" in which stars form, evolve, and die. The process of starbirth starts within these clouds, which are composed mainly of gas and dust. To help understand the properties of these clouds, we can compare them to fog on Earth. Fog is essentially a cloud at ground level, and just like fog, the amount of gas and dust in a space cloud can vary. This variation in density determines how we perceive these clouds. The exercise in identifying fog levels, from least to most, involves methods such as observing the clarity of light passing through the fog or noticing the degree to which objects are obscured by it. These methods also apply to studying space clouds, where the density and composition of the gas affect how much light passes through and how we detect them.


The Interstellar Medium (ISM) refers to the gas and dust found between stars in space. The ISM consists mainly of hydrogen and helium, with a small fraction (about 1%) of solid dust. Space clouds, like fog on Earth, affect how we see stars. The thickness of these clouds alters the visibility of stars, with denser clouds obscuring faint stars while allowing only brighter ones to be seen. This is similar to the way fog can block distant objects on Earth while allowing closer ones to remain visible. The appearance of space clouds is influenced by this small amount of dust, making them look very different from their actual gas composition.

Gas clouds, especially Giant Molecular Clouds (GMCs), are massive structures, some containing the equivalent mass of millions of suns. Despite their large size, the distance between particles in these clouds makes their gravity weak and the pressure inside them low. Using the ideal gas law, we understand that low pressure in these clouds results in low density and temperature. These clouds are so sparse that they are even less dense than Earth's atmosphere, and their temperature is only a few degrees above absolute zero. If our solar system were to pass through one of these clouds, the effects would be negligible, as the Sun and planets would remain unchanged, though distant stars would become obscured. The low energy in these clouds results in them releasing very little heat.


Gravitational contraction is the process by which gas clouds, unable to sustain fusion, shrink and become denser over time. In space, gas clouds that are not hot enough to initiate fusion, like the gas clouds in Orion, release their energy by emitting infrared light, primarily produced by dust rather than the gas itself. This occurs because the temperature of the gas is too low to produce visible light, which is emitted by hotter objects like stars. When these cold clouds lose energy, they begin to contract under their own gravity.

If two gas clouds collide, their material compresses, leading to an increase in density and a further reduction in size. Similarly, if a supernova explosion occurs nearby, the explosion compresses the gas cloud. In this scenario, the blast wave of the explosion pushes the near side of the cloud, causing it to move along with the shockwave, while the far side of the cloud remains unaffected until the shockwave reaches it. This process can create a compressed region within the cloud, causing it to shrink and become denser. Gravitational contraction, along with external forces like collisions or explosions, plays a key role in the evolution of gas clouds, leading to the eventual formation of stars.


Cloud fragmentation occurs when a gas cloud, under the influence of gravity, begins to contract unevenly. Despite the initial assumption of uniformity, the cloud is often not perfectly smooth, and certain regions may have slightly higher density than others. These denser areas experience stronger gravitational forces, pulling material towards them. As material accumulates in these regions, the gravitational force increases, causing more matter to be drawn in, while less dense areas lose material and become emptier. This process results in the fragmentation of the cloud into smaller clumps, each becoming a potential star. As these clumps of gas accumulate mass, their gravitational pull becomes stronger than the pressure opposing it. This causes them to shrink at an increasing rate, intensifying the gravitational force and accelerating the collapse. As the clumps shrink, their density and temperature rise rapidly. The temperature increases significantly due to the compressional heating from the increasing gravitational forces. When the core temperature of a clump reaches approximately 10 million degrees (Celsius or Kelvin), nuclear fusion begins, and the clump transforms into a star. This marks the birth of a star from the collapsing gas cloud.


When the gas clumps within a cloud shrink enough for fusion to occur, they officially become stars, transitioning from their prior state as "protostars" (Latin for "pre-star"). During this process, the cloud's material that remains surrounding the protostar begins to orbit, influenced by centrifugal force, which is stronger at the equator than at the poles. This force flattens the surrounding material into a disk shape. Within this disk, the 1% of solid particles from the original gas cloud begin to stick together, forming progressively larger solid chunks. These eventually become asteroids and planets, which form as a side effect during star formation. Therefore, while the universe focuses on creating stars, planets, like Earth, are also born from the same process.

The protostars that reach fusion first are the most massive, as they have stronger gravity, shrink faster, and heat up more quickly. These stars, known as O and B types, form before others due to their rapid development. Once O and B stars emerge, they begin emitting powerful radiation that ionizes the gas in the surrounding cloud, eroding it. This radiation increases pressure in the surrounding gas, eventually causing it to escape into space, effectively ending the cloud’s ability to form new stars. The stars' birth results in the destruction of their "nursery."

After the cloud has been dispersed, the remaining protostars, which will become lower mass stars (A, F, G, K, M types), continue to shrink and heat up until they reach fusion temperatures. These stars eventually join the main sequence of stars. The Sun, a G-type star, was prevented from becoming a more massive star because an O or B star in the neighborhood formed first, blowing away the remaining gas around our solar system. This prevented the formation of heavier elements but allowed the Sun to live long enough to support life on Earth, which ultimately became beneficial for the evolution of intelligent life.


The Main Sequence represents the first stage of a star's life, defined by the balance between pressure and gravity. This stage is characterized by a star's stable energy production, which results from the interaction between mass, gravity, pressure, and fusion. Massive stars, like O stars, have high mass, which generates strong gravitational forces. To balance this intense gravity, these stars must generate significant internal pressure, achieved by high temperatures. The high temperature causes atoms within the star to move rapidly, leading to frequent fusion reactions. Fusion occurs more readily at higher temperatures, so more fusion happens in hotter stars, producing significant amounts of energy and heat.

The energy produced by fusion must be released into space, a process known as luminosity. Therefore, more massive stars, which experience more fusion, are inherently hotter and more luminous. This explains why the Main Sequence forms a diagonal line on the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram, starting from the hot, luminous, and massive O stars, down to the cool, dim, and lightweight M stars. The Main Sequence is the most stable phase of a star's life, sustained by the balance between pressure and gravity (size balance) and fusion and luminosity (temperature balance).

The Main Sequence is the longest phase of a star's life, lasting about 90% of its total lifespan. For stars like our Sun, this stage can last around 11 billion years, while hotter, short-lived O stars may only spend about 1 million years in this stage. Cooler, long-lived M stars, on the other hand, can remain in the Main Sequence for up to 2 trillion years. Regardless of the star's size or lifespan, the Main Sequence is where it spends the majority of its existence. The failure of this stable phase marks the transition to the later, more unstable stages of a star's life, as it exhausts its fuel and struggles to maintain equilibrium.


At the end of the Main Sequence, a star faces a critical transition when it exhausts the Hydrogen in its core. While the outer layers still contain Hydrogen, it is too cold there for fusion to occur. The core, now composed mainly of Helium, cannot continue the same fusion process, as Helium fusion requires much higher temperatures than Hydrogen fusion. The difficulty arises because Helium atoms, having more protons than Hydrogen, experience stronger electromagnetic repulsion, making fusion more challenging. To overcome this, the atoms must be heated to much higher speeds, requiring temperatures approximately ten times higher—around 100 million Kelvin—compared to the 10 million Kelvin needed for Hydrogen fusion.

However, most stars, such as our Sun, are not hot enough in their outer layers to fuse Hydrogen or in their cores to initiate Helium fusion. As a result, the star can no longer maintain the stable fusion process that defined its Main Sequence phase. This marks the end of Stage 1 in the life of a star and leads to its transition into Stage 2, where it faces a new set of challenges and processes in its life cycle.


In Stage 2 of a low-mass star's life, such as our Sun, the star becomes a Red Giant after the core runs out of Hydrogen and fusion ceases in the core. As fusion stops in the core, the core begins to shrink due to gravitational contraction, which increases its density and temperature. This heating process eventually causes the outer layers of the star to heat up enough to start Hydrogen fusion in a shell surrounding the core. Fusion in the shell increases as the core continues to shrink and heat, leading to a rise in luminosity. This extra fusion energy must escape, creating an increase in light pressure, which eventually exceeds gravitational force.

This imbalance causes the outer layers of the star to expand significantly. As the star's outer layers expand and cool, the star's color shifts toward red, and it becomes a Red Giant. The Red Giant is characterized by an unstable configuration, with the core shrinking and heating up, while the outer layers expand, lose density, and cool down. This instability contrasts with the Main Sequence, where the star remains stable in size, temperature, and fusion rate.

The core of the Red Giant also experiences an increase in density, leading to the eventual onset of degenerate pressure. This occurs when the core becomes so dense that atoms are closely packed, allowing for efficient conduction of heat. As a result, the temperature of the entire core equalizes, causing the entire core to begin Helium fusion simultaneously, marking the end of Stage 2. This transition to Helium fusion is known as the Helium Flash and leads to the start of Stage 3 in the life cycle of the star.


Stage 3, the Horizontal Branch or Helium Main Sequence, marks a new phase for low-mass stars after the Helium Flash. The fusion of Helium in the core releases a burst of energy, raising the temperature and pressure. This causes the core to expand, reducing its density and temperature, and eliminating the importance of degenerate pressure. As a result, the shell, which had previously been heated by the core, cools down, reducing its fusion activity. With less fusion in the shell, the total fusion in the star decreases, leading to a reduction in light pressure. As light pressure diminishes, gravity once again dominates, causing the star to contract.

During this phase, the star reaches a stable, balanced state, similar to Stage 1. The core is fusing Helium into heavier elements, and the star is stable in size, temperature, and luminosity. This state is often referred to as the Helium Main Sequence because it behaves in the same way as the Hydrogen-fusing Main Sequence, but with Helium as the fuel. However, like the Main Sequence, Stage 3 will eventually end when the Helium in the core is exhausted, leading to a situation similar to what happened at the end of Stage 1. The core will no longer be able to sustain fusion, and the star will once again face a crisis, this time regarding its inability to fuse Carbon, which requires higher temperatures due to its heavier mass and charge.


Stage 4, the second Red Giant phase, mirrors Stage 2 in many ways. The core of the star contracts, becoming denser and hotter, reaching a point where degenerate pressure becomes significant. This contraction drives fusion in multiple shells around the core—one for Hydrogen fusion and one for Helium fusion. As these shells produce more energy, light pressure becomes significant once again, causing the outer layers of the star to expand. As the star expands, its density decreases and its temperature drops, causing the star to become a Red Giant once more, or more specifically, an asymptotic giant.

During this phase, the core attempts to reach Carbon fusion, but it cannot achieve this due to the instability caused by the ongoing fusion processes. The fusion is no longer contained deep within the core, and bursts of energy from the outer layers of the star can cause parts of the star to be ejected into space. This process leads to the creation of a planetary nebula, while the core, unable to sustain fusion, eventually cools into a white dwarf. This marks the end of a low-mass star’s life cycle, with Stage 4 being the final stage for stars like the Sun. These stars, having failed to reach Carbon fusion, will leave behind a white dwarf after shedding their outer layers.


Medium-mass stars, such as A, B, and O stars, follow a similar process to lower-mass stars, but on a more extreme scale due to their larger mass and greater gravitational pressure. Once these stars exhaust the Hydrogen in their cores, they enter a phase similar to the Red Giant phase observed in low-mass stars. However, because of their larger mass, these stars become Red Supergiants instead of regular giants. In Stage 3, these stars begin fusing Helium in their cores, becoming Blue Supergiants, which is analogous to the Horizontal Branch phase in lower-mass stars, but on a more intense scale.

For medium-mass stars, the process continues with additional stages. After Helium fusion, these stars can reach higher temperatures and pressures, allowing them to fuse Carbon in their cores, leading to Stage 5 (Carbon core). Stage 6 follows with Carbon shell fusion. This ability to fuse heavier elements like Carbon extends their lifecycle compared to lower-mass stars. For very massive stars, this process may continue, with the potential to fuse Neon if they are massive enough. However, for A-type stars, the end of Stage 6 marks their life cycle, culminating in the formation of a white dwarf after their outer layers are expelled to form a planetary nebula.

The brightest star in the night sky, Sirius, an A-type star, will ultimately follow this process, with its core evolving into a white dwarf while its outer layers are ejected into space. The life cycle of more massive stars will proceed differently, with even greater fusion capabilities.


High-mass stars, such as O and B stars, follow an extended and more complex fusion process compared to medium and low-mass stars. These stars can fuse multiple elements in their cores, adding extra stages of fusion as they progress toward the end of their lives. At the end of their lifetimes, these stars resemble an onion-like structure, with a series of concentric layers of different elements undergoing fusion. For example, in a star like Betelgeuse, the core would shrink to about the size of Earth, while maintaining a mass similar to that of the Sun. Its outer layers would stretch out to the orbit of Jupiter, and the star would emit about 100,000 times the luminosity of the Sun.

The lifetimes of these stages are drastically shorter compared to lower-mass stars. For a B-type star like Betelgeuse, the Hydrogen fusion stages last about 10 million years, Helium fusion lasts around 1 million years, Carbon fusion lasts about 1,000 years, Neon fusion lasts roughly 15 years, and Oxygen fusion lasts less than a year. If the star reaches the Silicon fusion stages, this would only last a day or less. Betelgeuse is thought to be in either stage 4 or stage 6, and if it is in stage 6, it could reach the end of its life within a very short time frame—potentially within our lifetime.

High-mass stars like Betelgeuse also shed their outer layers in a manner similar to the planetary nebulae seen in lower-mass stars, leading to clouds of expelled material. These clouds can obscure our view of the star and cause it to appear dimmer or change more rapidly than it actually is. While some astronomers believe that Betelgeuse is undergoing rapid changes, others suggest that the apparent rapid evolution may be due to material being ejected between the star and Earth, distorting our observations. The true stage of Betelgeuse’s life remains uncertain, but it is clear that high-mass stars have a more explosive and rapid progression towards their end compared to lower-mass stars.


When low and medium-mass stars reach the end of their life, their outer layers are expelled into space, forming a planetary nebula. This process contributes hydrogen and helium to the interstellar medium, which will eventually form the next generation of stars. The core, which once performed fusion, is left behind and becomes a white dwarf. This remnant cannot fuse any more elements, so it gradually loses energy and fades in luminosity, much like a dying ember. The white dwarf is composed of carbon and oxygen in low-mass stars, or oxygen, neon, and magnesium in medium-mass stars.

The Sun, a G2 main sequence star, is about 4.567 billion years old and has roughly 6 billion years remaining in its main sequence phase. Once the hydrogen in its core is exhausted, it will expand into a red giant. During this phase, it will grow large enough to engulf Mercury and Venus, vaporizing these planets. Earth will experience extreme temperatures, causing its oceans to evaporate and transforming it into a planet resembling Venus. The Sun will shed its outer layers over a billion years, leaving behind a core that will eventually become a white dwarf. This final white dwarf phase, which lasts for billions of years, will see the Sun slowly cool and fade away, while the remaining planets continue to orbit the dead star.

This process for low and medium-mass stars contrasts with the fate of high-mass stars, whose deaths involve more explosive ends.


When high-mass stars like O and B types (e.g., Betelgeuse) reach the end of their life cycle, their core becomes composed of iron, which signals the end of fusion. In stellar physics, fusion can only occur with elements lighter than iron, which fuse to release energy. Elements heavier than iron require fission to release energy. However, iron is the ultimate nuclear "waste" because both fusion and fission with iron absorb energy instead of releasing it. Thus, once a star's core is predominantly iron, it cannot sustain itself through nuclear reactions.

At this point, the star attempts to continue fusion through gravitational contraction, causing the core to become denser and hotter. The temperature rises to billions of degrees, and gamma rays are produced. Once the core reaches approximately 2 billion degrees, these gamma rays induce photodisintegration, where iron atoms are split back into hydrogen and helium. While this process uses energy, it doesn’t allow the star to continue fusion, leading to the collapse of the core. The collapse happens rapidly—within a fraction of a second—since the loss of pressure causes gravity to dominate. In about a tenth of a second, the core shrinks from the size of Earth to the size of a small town. The force of gravity is so intense that atoms and their subatomic particles merge, leading to the formation of a neutron star. The core is now composed entirely of neutrons, compressed into a singular atom the mass of a star but the size of a city.

This process triggers a supernova explosion, as the collapsing core’s energy release causes the outer layers of the star to violently eject. This catastrophic event marks the end of a massive star’s life, and the formation of a neutron star may result in one of the most energetic events in the universe.


The core-collapse supernova occurs when the core of a high-mass star, having collapsed into a neutron star, experiences a rapid bounce due to its motion energy. The collapse happens because the protons and electrons merge, leaving behind a singular, densely packed neutron core with no empty space. The outer layers of the star, which had been following the core’s collapse, continue inward until they collide with the bouncing core. This interaction transfers the energy from the core to the outer layers, causing them to explode outward. The implosion of the core generates the energy required for the explosion, leading to a supernova. This event results in an intense and chaotic release of energy, with temperatures reaching billions of degrees and atoms boiling as they explode outward in a violent, fast, and uneven manner.

As the blast wave rips through the star, it eventually reaches the surface. The star's surface, initially emitting light with the luminosity of about 100,000 suns at a few thousand degrees, is suddenly heated to millions of degrees, causing its luminosity to increase by a factor of 1,000, from 100,000 to 100 billion suns. The power of this explosion is so immense that it can outshine an entire galaxy. For example, the 1987A supernova, located 150,000 light years away, released energy that took 150,000 years to reach Earth, yet humans could still observe it with the naked eye. A supernova can be visible during the day, casting shadows and enabling people to read outside at night. While the Earth’s ozone layer would protect those on the surface from harmful radiation, astronauts in space would need to be cautious of electromagnetic pulses that could damage electronics. A supernova, such as the Crab Supernova Remnant from 1054, can take up to 15 years to cool and fade in luminosity, making it one of the most powerful and long-lasting events in the universe.


A Supernova Ia, or Type Ia supernova, differs from the core-collapse supernova. These events occur in binary star systems where one of the stars is a white dwarf. A white dwarf is the remnants of a low or medium mass star that has exhausted its nuclear fuel. In a binary system, the white dwarf can accumulate material from its companion star, typically a red giant, through accretion. When the white dwarf’s mass exceeds a critical limit (the Chandrasekhar limit, around 1.4 times the mass of the Sun), it can no longer support itself against gravitational collapse. This leads to a runaway thermonuclear reaction that triggers a violent explosion, resulting in a supernova. Supernova Ia events are important to astronomers as they serve as standard candles for measuring cosmic distances. The uniformity of their peak brightness allows astronomers to use them to calculate the distance to galaxies, providing key insights into the expansion of the universe.

Supernovae, including Type Ia, are incredibly hot events, with temperatures high enough to enable fusion in the outer layers of a star. This fusion produces heavier elements like Helium, Oxygen, Silicon, Carbon, and Calcium. These elements are ejected into space during the explosion, where they mix with interstellar gas clouds. The heavy elements, which form the building blocks of planets and life, become part of future star systems. This process is critical for the formation of planets and life, as stars create the elements essential for biology and geology. Without these stellar explosions, the elements required for life, such as iron for blood, calcium for bones, carbon for DNA, and oxygen for respiration, would not exist. The universe’s chemical evolution, driven by these explosive events, is integral to our existence. The story of astronomy is the story of our origins—everything we are, and everything we will be, is tied to the processes that occur in the universe.


After a star has exhausted its fuel, the remnants of its core are left behind, and these remnants are incredibly dense. For instance, a small amount of matter from a white dwarf or a neutron star could weigh as much as an entire skyscraper. These remnants are composed of extremely dense matter, and their size is so compact that the gravity produced by their mass is intense and doesn't fade quickly. In these objects, gravity is so strong that normal gas pressure cannot counterbalance it. This is where degenerate pressure comes into play.

Degenerate pressure is a unique force that helps prevent the collapse of these dense stellar remnants. It originates from quantum physics, where every particle, such as electrons, protons, and neutrons, must be distinguishable from one another. For example, if you place two electrons in the same space, their individual properties—such as energy or spin—become important. If two electrons have identical energy and spin and are forced into the same space, they cannot occupy the same position due to the Pauli Exclusion Principle. Instead, they "slip past" each other, creating an outward force. This resistance to compression is what we call degenerate pressure.

In white dwarfs, degenerate pressure is provided by electrons, which allows the star to maintain a balance between pressure and gravity, even though the atoms within the star are no longer relevant to the process. This allows white dwarfs to cool down over time without collapsing. Similarly, neutron stars, which are composed mainly of neutrons, rely on neutron degeneracy pressure to resist collapse. This pressure is independent of the temperature, so the star can remain stable despite its extreme density. Although this is a complex concept, degenerate pressure plays a crucial role in preventing both white dwarfs and neutron stars from collapsing under the immense force of gravity.


Black holes form when gravity becomes stronger than even the most powerful known pressure, degenerate pressure. Gravity has no limit, and if enough mass is concentrated in a small enough space, gravity can overpower degenerate pressure, causing the object to collapse continuously. This process results in what are called black holes, though the term was initially coined in a derogatory way to undermine their credibility. Over time, however, the name "black hole" became widely accepted.

Black holes are characterized by an escape velocity that exceeds the speed of light. Escape velocity is the speed an object must achieve to break free from a celestial body's gravity. On Earth, a bullet fired upwards doesn’t escape gravity because its speed is too slow, but a rocket can. The stronger the gravitational pull, the faster the escape velocity. However, when this velocity exceeds the speed of light, escape becomes impossible, which is why nothing can escape a black hole, not even light. This inability to escape is what gives black holes their "black" nature.

The boundary beyond which nothing can escape a black hole is called the event horizon. Within this zone, the escape velocity surpasses the speed of light, ensuring that nothing—light, matter, or radiation—can break free. Outside the event horizon, the gravity weakens with distance, and escape is theoretically possible. If the Sun were replaced by a black hole of the same mass, Earth would continue orbiting it normally but would freeze due to the lack of sunlight. The real effects of black holes become evident as you approach the event horizon. Close to a black hole, gravity distorts space itself, making it impossible to maintain a stable orbit.

An interesting phenomenon occurs near black holes due to the intense difference in gravitational pull at different points. If you were to approach a black hole feet first, the gravity at your feet would be stronger than at your head, causing your body to stretch. This stretching, which happens as an object is pulled apart by a black hole’s gravity, is called spaghettification. This process, where an object is stretched into long, thin strands, is one of the most dramatic and lethal effects of black holes.


To study the life cycle of stars, we need to observe them through their birth, life, and death stages. However, a major challenge arises because even the shortest-lived stars, such as O-type stars, live for about a million years. This presents a problem for human scientists, who typically have a much shorter lifespan and tend to lose interest in things that take longer than a few decades to change.

This problem can be understood through a simple analogy. Imagine you are a mosquito, which only lives for about a week. If you wanted to understand the life cycle of humans, you would have to find a way to observe their life stages despite the vast difference in lifespan. Simply watching a human for your entire life wouldn't allow you to directly measure their aging process. The solution lies in finding ways to observe multiple stages of the long-lived entity in a short-lived timeframe, such as by studying star clusters, where stars of different ages exist together. This allows scientists to study various life stages of stars within a human lifetime, providing insights into their evolution.


To understand the life cycle of stars, astronomers have developed a method similar to how a short-lived mosquito might observe the long life cycles of humans. Just as the mosquito could categorize humans by age (from babies to the elderly) by observing multiple individuals, astronomers study stars of various ages to piece together the evolution of stars. This process is essential because stars, especially the shortest-lived ones like O-type stars, have lifespans far exceeding human observation, lasting millions of years. Thus, by observing groups of stars with known formation histories, astronomers can deduce their age and the stages they go through.

This method leads to the study of star clusters, groups of stars that all formed from the same gas cloud. Since the stars in a cluster share a common origin, they are all roughly the same age and composed of the same elements, allowing astronomers to better understand the life cycle of stars. The age of a star can be estimated by knowing its type, as different stars (like O, B, A, F, G, K, and M) have varying lifespans based on their mass. For instance, O stars live for millions of years, while M stars may live for billions. This method of studying star clusters gives valuable insight into stellar evolution because every star in the cluster was born at the same time.

Our solar system, however, is not currently in a star cluster. It was originally part of a cluster but was ejected when something caused the cluster to break apart. The solar system was flung into a different part of the Milky Way galaxy. Despite this, astronomers have discovered a sibling star from the same parent cloud that once formed our solar system, showing that we were indeed part of a larger stellar community. Understanding star clusters and their role in the formation of stars is crucial for understanding stellar lifecycles and the evolution of our galaxy.


The Main Sequence Turn-off method helps determine the age of a star cluster based on the types of stars present and missing. In a cluster, the shortest-lived stars die off first, and the longer-lived ones remain. For example, in a young cluster, you might still find O-type stars, which live for only a million years. If O stars are present, the cluster must be younger than a million years. In a slightly older cluster, O and B stars are absent, but A, F, G, K, and M stars remain. Since B stars live for about 10 million years, the cluster must be older than that but younger than 100 million years, as A stars would still be visible in a cluster younger than that. A third example of an older cluster shows the absence of O, B, A, and F stars, with only G, K, and M stars present. F stars live for a billion years, so the cluster is older than that, but G stars, which live for 10 billion years, prevent the cluster from being older than 10 billion years.

This method is useful for estimating the age of star clusters by examining the stars that are still present and those that have disappeared. It cannot be used for individual stars but only for collections of stars, like star clusters. The method works because the lifespan of different stars is predictable, and their presence or absence provides a timeline for the cluster’s age.


The composition of star clusters can be used to test theories about star formation and aging. Open clusters and globular clusters have different elemental compositions. Open clusters have about 70% hydrogen, 28% helium, and 2% heavy elements, while globular clusters have a slightly higher percentage of hydrogen (76%) and a much lower percentage of heavy elements (0.01%). Astronomers categorize elements into three categories: hydrogen, helium, and heavy elements, which include everything else.

Fusion in stars produces helium and heavy elements. The more fusion that occurs, the more helium and heavy elements are created, and the less hydrogen remains. However, the observed data only reflects the composition of the outer layers of stars, not their cores. For example, the Sun’s core has already consumed almost half of its hydrogen, but this is not reflected in the observed data, which only measures the outer layers. The outer layers of stars contain the fossil record of their original composition, which is the composition of the gas cloud from which the star formed.

Fusion inside stars doesn't significantly affect the distribution of elements in space. The heavy elements we observe in the universe are mostly produced by supernova explosions, which release these elements into space. Early in the universe’s history, no supernovae had occurred, so heavy elements were scarce. Later generations of stars formed from gas clouds enriched with heavy elements from previous supernovae. Thus, heavy elements can be used as a "clock" to estimate a star’s age. Stars formed early in the universe’s history, when heavy elements were scarce, are much older than those formed in later generations, when heavy elements were abundant.

The composition of stars in a cluster can be used in combination with other methods, like the Main Sequence Turn-off, to understand the age and history of galaxies. Understanding the presence and abundance of heavy elements allows astronomers to track the generations of stars and their evolution over time.


Star clusters are born in gas clouds, but their location depends on the type of cluster. Some star clusters are found only next to gas clouds, while others can be found throughout space. This distinction can be compared to a thought experiment involving a bug that must be born in the ocean. If the bug could live anywhere, its lifespan would affect where it could be found. For example, a bug that lives for a week might not be found in a place like Colorado, but one that lives a year could be found in both Malibu and Colorado. Similarly, star clusters have different locations based on their characteristics.

Astronomers have used this kind of logic to identify the types of stars that produce different kinds of supernovae. Core-collapse supernovae are found near gas clouds, while Type Ia supernovae can occur anywhere. This pattern suggests that the stars involved in core-collapse supernovae are likely to be born in or near gas clouds, whereas the stars responsible for Type Ia supernovae are not as dependent on their location. The difference in their locations helps determine the kind of star that undergoes each type of supernova.


Our galaxy consists of hundreds of billions of solar systems, each with its own star and planets. To understand the structure of the Milky Way, we need to determine the distances to various parts of it. One method for measuring these distances is parallax, which involves observing the apparent shift in the position of a star as Earth orbits the Sun. The closer an object is, the more noticeable its parallax, and the farther away it is, the less parallax we observe. However, for very distant objects, the parallax is so small that it becomes difficult to measure, limiting the effectiveness of this method.

Currently, the European satellite Gaia is measuring the parallax of about 2 billion stars, which seems impressive. However, in the context of the Milky Way, which contains around 200 billion stars, this means only about 1% of the galaxy has been measured. This small sample size makes it challenging to fully understand the structure of our galaxy. To measure distances to objects farther away, astronomers need other methods, such as luminosity distances, which will be discussed next.


Luminosity distances are determined by the relationship between an object's luminosity, its brightness, and its distance. We can measure an object's brightness by capturing its light and comparing it to its known luminosity. The brightness observed from Earth helps us estimate how far away the object is. If the object is faint but known to be dim in luminosity, it must be close. If the object is faint but known to be luminous, it must be far away, as light diminishes over distance. However, this method requires prior knowledge of the object's luminosity. If we know the luminosity in advance, we refer to the object as a "Standard Candle" and can use this method to determine its distance.

The first standard candle is simple: stars. By analyzing the spectral lines of a star, we can determine its temperature and size, which in turn gives us its luminosity. This method works because stars are relatively straightforward objects, and their luminosities are well-understood, especially when placed on the Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram.

The second standard candle involves a more complex type of star: Periodic Variable Stars. These stars are unstable, but their instability is periodic, meaning their changes follow a regular, predictable pattern. The brightness variations are caused by the stars' pulsations, which occur because of an imbalance between pressure and gravity. When pressure becomes too high, the star expands, lowering its density, temperature, and pressure. Eventually, gravity overcomes the pressure, causing the star to contract, increasing its density, temperature, and pressure again. This cycle of expansion and contraction causes the star's brightness to fluctuate. The length of the cycle depends on the star's size: smaller stars undergo rapid pulsations, while larger stars take longer to complete their cycles. By measuring the period of these pulsations, we can determine the star's average size and luminosity.

The third standard candle, which the reader is asked to look up, is another method used for determining luminosity and distance. These standard candles are essential tools for measuring distances to objects in the galaxy, and they help astronomers understand the vast distances in space.


The Milky Way is the galaxy that contains our solar system, and understanding its structure is key to understanding our place in the universe. The Milky Way appears as a fuzzy band in the sky, a ring that can be seen stretching across the heavens. It wasn't until Galileo, using a telescope, that humanity discovered this band was not just a blur but a collection of billions of stars. By observing the sky through his telescope, Galileo helped shift our perception, allowing us to see individual stars that were once just part of a vague light.

This concept of observing the stars to understand our galaxy's shape works in a similar way to how we might perceive a city from a distance. If you are on a rooftop and see a few lights scattered around, you likely live in a small town. However, if you're in a large city like Los Angeles, the lights appear dense and abundant. By analyzing the distribution of stars in the sky, we can figure out where we are in the galaxy. If the stars are evenly spread out in all directions, we know we are at the center. If more stars are visible in one direction than another, we are likely near the edge of the galaxy. This spatial distribution helps us infer the shape of our galaxy.

From our vantage point, the Milky Way appears large and full of stars in the central band, with fewer stars visible above and below that plane. This suggests that the galaxy has a thin, disk-like shape, with most stars concentrated in the plane of the disk, and fewer stars above or below it. This model of the galaxy's structure is consistent with how we observe the Milky Way stretching across the sky.

In 1785, astronomers such as Caroline Herschel and her brother William mapped the stars in the galaxy. Their map, based on star counts in various directions, revealed that we are located near the center of the galaxy. The map also showed dark regions along the Milky Way, which were initially thought to indicate gaps in the galaxy. However, as astronomers later realized, these dark spots were caused by gas clouds that obstructed our view of the stars behind them, rather than empty space.

The modern view of the Milky Way continues to be refined with new observations and technologies, allowing us to better understand the true structure of our galaxy. However, it is important to remember that the historical belief of being at the center of the universe has been challenged and corrected over time, and our place within the Milky Way is just one of many galaxies in the vast universe.


In the early 1900s, astronomers began using globular clusters to better understand the structure of our galaxy. These clusters, located far from the gas clouds of the Milky Way, were not obstructed by the clouds and could be used to determine the direction of the galaxy's center. By counting the distribution of these clusters, astronomers concluded that the center of the galaxy lies in the direction with the highest concentration of stars, revealing that we are located some distance from the galactic center. This led to the development of the modern view of the Milky Way.

The modern map of the Milky Way consists of several key components. The most significant part of our galaxy is the Disk, which is wide around the equator but thin from north to south. The disk contains about 90% of the galaxy's stars, including the Sun, and is also home to spiral arms and blue stars. Blue stars are short-lived and are found near gas clouds, where new stars are born. While the disk contains both young and old stars, the young stars are more visible due to their blue color, while the older stars are less noticeable.

At the center of the galaxy is the Bulge, a rounded region that contains about 10% of the galaxy's stars. Unlike the disk, the bulge lacks visible gas clouds, and as a result, it is populated mainly by older, redder stars. The absence of blue stars suggests that the bulge is too old to produce new stars. The structure of the Milky Way, with its disk and bulge, often reminds observers of a flying saucer shape.

Beyond the disk and bulge, the galaxy also contains a Halo, a large, spherical region that is much larger than the other components. However, the halo contains very few stars—only about 0.5% of the galaxy's total—and is the emptiest part of the Milky Way. There are no gas clouds in the halo, and it is primarily composed of very old stars. While the halo seems insignificant at first, its role in galaxy collisions and its potential connection to dark matter will become more apparent in future studies.

Finally, at the center of the galaxy lies the galactic center, an important and complex region of the Milky Way.


The Galactic Center is the focal point of the Milky Way galaxy, located at the center of the disk, the bulge, and the halo. However, due to the dense gas and dust surrounding this region, observing it directly is challenging. To see through these obstacles, astronomers use x-ray or infrared vision. When they do, they uncover something extraordinary: stars orbiting around a central point, adhering to Kepler's Laws of planetary motion. One such star, labeled SO-2, moves closer and farther from the center, following predictable orbital patterns.

The importance of these observations lies in the fact that Kepler's Laws are governed by gravity, which allows astronomers to estimate the mass exerted by the central object. By studying the orbits of these stars, it has been determined that the central object of the galaxy has a mass equivalent to about 4 million times that of the Sun. However, despite the mass, there is no visible light emanating from this spot. This leads to the conclusion that the object at the center is not emitting light, but rather has significant gravitational influence. The answer is a supermassive black hole.

It turns out that every galaxy measured so far contains a supermassive black hole at its center. While the existence of these black holes has been a subject of mystery for decades, recent research is shedding light on their functioning, origin, and broader implications. This work has made significant advances, including Dr. Andrea Ghez’s Nobel Prize-winning contributions related to the galactic center. The study of supermassive black holes remains crucial to understanding the dynamics and evolution of galaxies.


Galaxies are vast collections of stars, gas, and dust held together by gravity. The understanding of galaxies, like many scientific concepts, developed gradually over time as observations became clearer and theories evolved. Early scientific progress often involved confronting ambiguous evidence and asking questions about what was observed. A prime example of this process occurred when astronomers were trying to understand the nature of spiral objects observed in the sky, such as the Crab Nebula and the Whirlpool Galaxy.

In the 1850s, astronomers only had access to the best telescopes of the time, which could provide limited, unclear images. The Crab Nebula, a remnant of a supernova, and the Whirlpool Galaxy were both observed as fuzzy, spiral-like blobs. These sketches, made with the largest telescope of the era, led to two competing ideas. One theory suggested that these spiral shapes were simply gas clouds, part of the Milky Way galaxy, making the Milky Way the only galaxy in existence. The other theory proposed that these spirals could be distant galaxies, much like our own, implying that the universe was much larger than previously thought.

The debate over the nature of these objects hinged on one critical piece of information: distance. By measuring the distance to these objects, astronomers could determine whether they were part of the Milky Way or separate galaxies altogether. This question of distance was crucial in resolving the larger question of the scale of the universe. Early observations and sketches from the best telescopes of the time, like those at Birr, Ireland, set the stage for later advancements that would reshape our understanding of galaxies and the universe.


The key to resolving the debate about whether certain spiral objects in space were gas clouds or separate galaxies lies in determining their distance. Distance provides crucial insights into their luminosity, size, and location, each of which helps us understand their true nature.

Luminosity refers to the brightness of an object. If the spirals were nearby, their light would not have had the chance to spread out, making them appear faint, like the luminosity of a single solar system. This could align with the idea that they are gas clouds, potentially forming solar systems. However, if these spirals were far away, their brightness would be immense, likely equivalent to billions of suns. The only way to produce such luminosity is for the object to contain billions of stars, meaning these spirals must be galaxies.

The size of these objects further supports the distance-based conclusion. If they were close, they would appear small, perhaps only the size of a solar system, which would make sense if they were gas clouds. However, if they are far away, they must be much larger, potentially as large as the Milky Way. The size of the object, therefore, suggests that these spirals are galaxies.

Finally, the location of these objects is important. If something is within the same space as we are, it is likely associated with us. For example, if a cat is one arm's distance from me, it is probably my pet. But if the cat is seven miles away, it is less likely to be mine. Similarly, if a spiral is within our galaxy, it is likely a gas cloud. If it is far away, it is its own galaxy. By determining the distance to these spirals, astronomers could confirm whether they are gas clouds or separate galaxies.


In the early 1900s, astronomers were able to resolve the debate about the nature of spiral objects, thanks to the work of Henrietta Leavitt. Leavitt discovered that Cepheid variable stars, a type of pulsating star, could be used as "standard candles" to measure distances in space. This discovery provided a way for astronomers to determine the luminosity and distance of celestial objects. Without her work, the question of whether spiral objects were gas clouds or separate galaxies would have remained unanswered.

Shortly after Leavitt's breakthrough, Edwin Hubble applied her method to the Andromeda spiral, the closest spiral galaxy to the Milky Way. Using the periodic variable stars, Hubble determined that Andromeda was more than ten times the size of the Milky Way, placing it far outside our galaxy. This was a key finding because it confirmed that Andromeda was not just a gas cloud but an entirely separate galaxy. With this discovery, Hubble helped astronomers understand that the universe is filled with galaxies, making it astronomically vast. This finding was pivotal in expanding our understanding of the universe, and it is fitting that the Hubble Space Telescope was named in his honor.


Once it was established that spiral objects were galaxies, the next step was to classify and understand their structures. Hubble's work in this area led to the identification of two main types of galaxy shapes: flat disks, which are Spiral Galaxies, and rounded balls, which are Elliptical Galaxies. There were also some galaxies with no clear shape, known as Irregular Galaxies. Hubble created a diagram, called the Tuning Fork diagram, to categorize these shapes, but we can focus on the two main types.

Elliptical galaxies are characterized by their lack of structure. They appear as smooth, featureless, and uniform, without any visible gas clouds, star formation, or spiral arms. Their shape can range from more rounded to more oblong, and they do not show variations in light like spiral galaxies do. This lack of distinguishing features makes them relatively unremarkable compared to other galaxy types. Some elliptical galaxies can appear more rounded, while others are elongated.


Spiral galaxies have more complex and visually intriguing structures compared to elliptical galaxies. One of the key features in their classification is the relationship between the size of the central bulge and the tightness of the spiral arms. In Sa galaxies, the bulge is prominent, containing a large number of stars, although still fewer than in the disk. The spiral arms in these galaxies are tightly wound, making it difficult to distinguish where one arm ends and another begins. On the other hand, Sc galaxies, such as the Milky Way, have a small bulge and loosely wound arms that can be traced from the center to the edge of the disk. Sb galaxies, like the Andromeda Galaxy, sit between these two extremes, with a moderate-sized bulge and spiral arms that are neither too tight nor too loose.

In addition to the standard spiral galaxies, there are also barred spiral galaxies, which follow a similar pattern in terms of bulge size and arm tightness, but with an important difference: they have a bar-like structure passing through the center. This bar distinguishes them from non-barred spirals, even though their overall spiral arm structure is similar. The Milky Way is classified as an SBc galaxy, which means it is a barred spiral galaxy with loosely wound arms and a small bulge. Barred spiral galaxies are common, making up the majority of the well-sized galaxies in the universe.

While the focus has been on spiral and barred spiral galaxies, it's important to mention irregular galaxies as well. These galaxies do not have a clear, defined structure, and they do not fit into the traditional classifications of spirals or ellipticals.


Irregular galaxies are the least structured of all galaxy types. They lack a clear shape or defined form, appearing as jumbled blobs of stars and gas clouds. These galaxies do not follow the spiral or elliptical classifications and are considered chaotic in structure. While they are often referred to as "clouds," this term is a historical artifact, as astronomers tend to keep old naming conventions without updating them.

Thus, irregular galaxies stand apart from the two main galaxy types—spirals and ellipticals—and are categorized as "none of the above," existing as amorphous, disorganized systems. Before transitioning to other topics, it’s worth noting that different galaxy types host different star populations and structures, which will be explored further.


Galaxies are classified by their shape, and this classification also reveals important information about their composition. Each type of galaxy tends to contain specific elements and star types, which are fairly consistent across galaxies of the same shape.

Spiral galaxies typically contain about 20% gas clouds. They host a variety of stars, including blue and red main sequence stars, supergiants, giants, and white dwarfs. The stars in spiral galaxies generally have low to medium amounts of heavy elements, with stars like the Sun falling into the latter category.

Elliptical galaxies, in contrast, contain no gas clouds. Their stellar population is made up mostly of red main sequence stars, giants, and white dwarfs. These galaxies have stars with a range of heavy elements, from low to high amounts. This suggests a more evolved stellar population compared to spirals.

Irregular galaxies are dominated by gas clouds, which make up around 90% of their composition. They also contain blue and red main sequence stars, along with supergiants, but few giants. The stars in irregular galaxies have low amounts of heavy elements, which reflects their younger and less evolved stellar populations.

These differences in galaxy composition raise important questions about star formation and the life cycle of stars. Gas clouds in galaxies play a critical role in star formation, with the presence or absence of heavy elements influencing the types of stars that can form. Heavy elements, which are created through nuclear fusion in stars, are released into galaxies when stars die, enriching the surrounding space. Galaxies with fewer heavy elements tend to be younger, with stars that are still in their early stages of development. These patterns help astronomers understand not only the composition of different galaxy types but also their evolutionary processes.


Spheroids, which include elliptical galaxies and the bulges of spiral galaxies, are shaped by gravity and pressure. These rounded structures are formed because of the randomness in the motion of the stars and gas clouds within them. Just like gas clouds, spheroids are made up of stars that move in random directions, creating a rounded shape. In these galaxies, gravity and pressure work together to maintain stability, similar to how gas clouds are held together by pressure counteracting the force of gravity.

Pressure in spheroids is a result of density and temperature. Higher-density spheroids have stars that are closer together, creating more pressure to balance out the stronger gravitational pull. For example, massive elliptical galaxies, with their greater mass, have stronger gravity and require higher pressure to maintain stability, leading to a higher density of stars. Conversely, a less massive spiral bulge, with weaker gravity, has lower pressure and therefore a lower density, with stars more spread out.

Temperature, in this analogy, is related to the motion of the stars, similar to how temperature affects the motion of atoms in a gas. A massive elliptical galaxy, needing greater pressure to balance its gravity, has stars that move faster, creating a higher "temperature." A low-mass spiral bulge, with weaker gravity and lower pressure, has stars that move more slowly, resulting in a lower "temperature." This concept of pressure and temperature applies in the same way to spheroids as it does to gas clouds, with stars in these galaxies moving in random directions, similar to atoms in a gas.


The disks of spiral galaxies are highly ordered, with all parts moving in the same direction, similar to the disk of our solar system. Gravity tries to pull objects inward, but their sideways motion causes them to orbit around the galaxy instead of falling directly toward the center. This is explained by centripetal force, which ensures the objects remain in orbit rather than spiraling in. The mass of the galaxy influences the speed of these orbits: more massive galaxies have their stars and gas clouds orbiting faster. This relationship is critical because it allows astronomers to measure the mass of any galaxy using the Doppler effect to detect the motion of its stars, though irregular galaxies, with their chaotic structures, are an exception.

Additionally, gravity's effect on the orbiting motion in galaxies can be described using Kepler's Laws. According to these laws, as gravity weakens with distance from a mass, objects farther from the center of a galaxy should orbit slower. However, observations of galaxy rotation do not match this expectation. The green line in the rotation curve of galaxies represents what we would expect from gravity alone, while the yellow line shows the actual observations, revealing much higher speeds at greater distances from the galaxy’s center. This discrepancy suggests the presence of unseen mass exerting additional gravitational pull, leading to the hypothesis of dark matter, which will be discussed further next week.


The primary distinction between galaxies and solar systems lies in their complexity. A solar system, like ours, is much simpler because most of its mass is concentrated in a single object, the Sun, which accounts for 99.8% of the solar system's mass and gravity. The planets, asteroids, and comets orbit the Sun, which makes the system relatively easy to model mathematically. In contrast, galaxies are far more complex. While each galaxy contains a supermassive black hole at its center, this black hole's gravity is not the dominant force. For example, the Milky Way's supermassive black hole has about four million times the mass of the Sun, but it is vastly outmatched by the mass of the galaxy's 200 billion stars, which make up the majority of the galaxy's gravitational influence. As a result, stars in the Milky Way experience more gravitational pull from other stars than from the black hole, even though the Sun still orbits the galaxy's center.

In a galaxy, the gravitational forces from all stars and gas clouds are more distributed. This means that any given point, such as a red dot within the galaxy, feels gravitational pull from different directions, but these forces collectively pull the point toward the galaxy's center. This results in stars orbiting the galactic center. To understand the dynamics of galaxy formation, astronomers use computer simulations. These simulations, driven by graphics cards (initially optimized for video games like first-person shooters), calculate how gravity works across millions of objects, each exerting forces on one another. These computational advancements have been crucial in studying complex structures like spiral arms in galaxies.


Spiral arms in galaxies are regions where new stars are born, and they appear blue because of the presence of hot, young stars. These stars, particularly O-type stars, have short lifespans, typically around one million years, so they don't travel far from their birthplace within the spiral arms. In contrast, our solar system takes about 200 million years to orbit the Milky Way, which means that when the solar system was in this same part of the galaxy, T. rex had not yet evolved. Thus, the blue stars we see in spiral arms mark regions of recent star formation. Red main sequence stars, although born in the spiral arms, are dimmer and less noticeable, and the space between the arms is filled with these stars, which are obscured by the brighter blue ones.

The formation of spiral arms is an example of an emergent property. This concept refers to a complex behavior arising from simple rules applied to many components. Just as ants form complex colonies and brain cells form the consciousness, gravity and the motion of stars create the spiral arms. A spiral arm isn't a fixed "thing" but rather a pattern resulting from the orbits of stars and gas clouds. Stars are born within spiral arms but don't stay there forever; they move in orbits that bring them in and out of these arms. The spiral arm is, therefore, an optical effect that arises from the interactions and orbits of numerous stars and gas clouds.

While stars in spiral arms do not collide due to their relatively vast distances, gas clouds are much more likely to interact. When gas clouds collide, they can compress and trigger the formation of new stars. This process is most likely to happen within the crowded spiral arms, where gas clouds are more densely packed. Consequently, most stars are formed in these regions of spiral arms, where the density of gas clouds increases the likelihood of star formation.


Bars in spiral galaxies are patterns of star orbits that resemble spiral arms but differ in their function. While spiral arms are regions of star formation, bars are formed from a family of orbits where stars are continually nudged in the same direction by the gravitational influence of neighboring stars. This persistent influence causes stars to eventually align with the bar's orbit, and over time, the bar grows as more stars join. The bar is not a stable structure; as it becomes populated, its gravitational forces grow stronger, and the stars within it experience gravitational slingshots, sending them off in random directions. This process leads to the self-destruction of the bar.

As the bar dissipates, it contributes to the formation of a bulge in the galaxy. This cyclical process occurs throughout the galaxy’s life, starting with a simple disk, evolving into a galaxy with a strong bar, and then transitioning to a self-destructive phase where the bulge accumulates more stars. This cycle repeats, progressively building up the bulge, leading to different galaxy types, such as Sc, SBc, Sb, SBb, and eventually Sa or SBa galaxies. The bar's role in transferring stars from the disk to the bulge reflects an evolutionary sequence, shaping the galaxy's structure over time. However, not all galaxies will necessarily complete this sequence, and we will explore why in the next section.


Galaxies are not isolated in space; instead, they are part of larger structures and collections. The Hubble Deep Field image, a significant photograph from the Hubble Space Telescope, reveals this cosmic arrangement. The image shows galaxies spread across the universe, with only a few stars from our own Milky Way visible. These stars are identifiable by the spikes emanating from them, but the rest of the light sources in the image are distant galaxies, each containing billions of stars and their respective solar systems. This image serves to emphasize the immense scale and density of the universe, highlighting that galaxies are abundant, even in areas that seem dark to the naked eye.

Galaxies are not evenly distributed throughout the universe. Instead, they form groups and clusters. Our Milky Way and the nearby Andromeda Galaxy are part of a group known as the Local Group, which consists of about 30 galaxies. Larger clusters exist as well, containing thousands of galaxies, all gravitationally bound and orbiting each other. These clusters are part of even larger structures called superclusters, which consist of groups of clusters connected in vast cosmic strings. In contrast to these crowded areas, there are regions called voids, where galaxies are sparse or entirely absent. The proximity of galaxies to each other leads to frequent interactions, including collisions, which will be explored further in the following sections.


Galaxies, due to their proximity to one another, frequently collide. The reason galaxies collide while stars generally do not is because the relative distance between stars is much greater than the size of the stars themselves. To understand this, consider the analogy of two blindfolded mosquitos versus two blindfolded elephants in a room: a meter of space between mosquitos is significant, but for elephants, it is relatively small. Galaxies are closer together in relation to their size compared to stars, making collisions between galaxies inevitable.

As galaxies approach each other, one of the first effects they experience is tidal forces. Tides arise because gravity weakens with distance, causing stronger gravitational pull on the nearer side of an object than on the farther side. This results in a stretching effect, where the near side of the galaxy is pulled more strongly than the far side. Even if galaxies are not physically touching, they still exert tidal forces on each other, distorting their shapes. However, the impact of these tidal forces depends on the mass of the galaxies involved. Both galaxies experience equal amounts of tidal force, as dictated by Newton's Third Law, but the galaxy with less mass is more affected because it has less resistance to the gravitational pull.

When galaxies of unequal mass interact, the larger galaxy typically dominates, leading to one-sided outcomes like galactic cannibalism. The larger galaxy tends to strip away material from the smaller one, pulling it apart over time. The Milky Way, for instance, has been a dominant force in the Local Group for billions of years, illustrating the aggressive nature of larger galaxies in such interactions. This imbalance in power between galaxies is a prime example of the harsh dynamics in the universe, where the larger, more massive galaxies often prevail in these gravitational interactions.


Ram Pressure Stripping is a process that occurs when a greater galaxy exerts pressure on a lesser galaxy, stripping away its gas. This occurs when the lesser galaxy moves through the halo of the greater galaxy, which contains low-density gas. As the lesser galaxy moves through this halo, the interaction creates a "wind" that forces the gas in the lesser galaxy backwards. The lesser galaxy, lacking the gravitational strength to retain its gas, loses it to the greater galaxy. This loss is catastrophic for the lesser galaxy, as gas clouds are necessary for star formation. Without gas, the galaxy cannot produce new stars, leading to its eventual decline.

A galaxy that stops forming stars is considered "dead" in astronomical terms. The absence of new stars means that the galaxy's stars are no longer being replenished. As the blue stars, which are younger and more energetic, die off, the galaxy becomes "red and dead," with only older, redder stars remaining. This is the inevitable fate of a lesser galaxy subjected to ram pressure stripping.

The greater galaxy, however, benefits from this process. The gas stripped from the lesser galaxy may be absorbed into the halo of the greater galaxy. As this gas undergoes gravitational contraction, it becomes denser and eventually forms new gas clouds that can contribute to star formation in the greater galaxy. This process allows the greater galaxy to continue thriving while the lesser galaxy fades.

However, even the dominant galaxies are not exempt from such processes. When a greater galaxy enters a galaxy cluster, it can encounter an even larger halo and experience ram pressure stripping itself. This can turn a once-active galaxy, like the Milky Way, into a "red and dead" spiral galaxy, also known as an S0 galaxy. These galaxies are no longer forming new stars and eventually become passive, their once-vibrant disks now devoid of young stars. Despite the stripping of their gas, the galaxies may retain their spiral structure, though without the blue stars that once characterized them.


When a lesser galaxy approaches a greater galaxy, it can be torn apart as it enters the star-filled regions of the greater galaxy. The stars in the lesser galaxy are not directly destroyed during these collisions. Instead, they are often freed from the gravitational influence of their original galaxy and become part of the greater galaxy's system, entering into independent orbits. These stars are referred to as tidal streams, and the Milky Way contains more than twenty tidal streams from galaxies it has captured and torn apart over time.

The stars in these tidal streams contribute to the Milky Way’s halo. This phenomenon is called galactic cannibalism, where a larger galaxy "eats" a smaller one, absorbing its stars and other matter. While the stars themselves are not harmed, the lesser galaxy is effectively destroyed, its stars scattered into the greater galaxy’s system. The Milky Way, as a dominant galaxy, has engaged in this process multiple times, capturing and disrupting smaller galaxies.

However, the Milky Way is not immune to this process. It is on a collision course with the Andromeda galaxy, a galaxy of similar size. When this collision occurs, the Milky Way will face its own fate as it merges with Andromeda, a process that will not bode well for our galaxy. This event will lead to significant changes for both galaxies as they interact, merging their stars and gas, and forming a new galactic structure.


When two equal galaxies collide, neither can use its mass to resist the effects of the other. Both galaxies are torn apart, and the resulting debris forms a new galaxy. This process is known as a galaxy merger. Despite the disruption, the stars themselves are not directly affected by the collision, although they may be flung around in different orbits. The collision is similar to two clouds of gnats flying into each other; the individual stars within the galaxies don't collide with each other, even though the galaxies themselves do.

However, gas clouds in the galaxies are a different matter. When gas clouds from different galaxies collide, the impact is far more significant. Unlike clouds within a single galaxy, where gas clouds may move together slowly, clouds from different galaxies collide head-on. This intense collision creates a highly dense region where star formation happens rapidly, often within just a few thousand years. The density of the resulting gas cloud is so high that it can convert a large portion—up to 98%—of the gas into new stars. In comparison, typical gas clouds within galaxies might only convert about 1% of their mass into stars before the formation process is halted by the newly formed stars blowing away the remaining gas.

Moreover, the collision of gas clouds during a galaxy merger favors the creation of heavier stars. Instead of having just a few hundred massive stars, a merging galaxy might create millions of O and B stars. This phenomenon, known as a starburst, makes the galaxy appear exceptionally blue. However, this burst of star formation is short-lived. The O and B stars rapidly exhaust their fuel and go supernova, causing massive explosions that, in turn, expel most of the remaining gas from the galaxy. This leaves the galaxy without enough gas to continue forming stars, and the blue stars gradually die off without replacement, turning the galaxy red and dead over time.

The result of such a merger is a galaxy with no remaining gas for further star formation, and with many of its newly formed stars gone supernova. These explosions enrich the galaxy with heavy elements that were created during the supernova events.


An Active Galactic Nucleus (AGN) occurs when gas from a galaxy is drawn toward its supermassive black hole at the galaxy's center. Normally, the black hole sits quietly, with stars orbiting it without significant interactions. However, when gas gets pulled into the black hole's gravitational field, it undergoes intense gravitational contraction. As the gas is pulled deeper into the black hole's gravity, it becomes extremely hot due to the immense forces involved. This heat causes the gas to emit light across various wavelengths, from infrared to visible light, ultraviolet, and even x-rays. The luminosity can reach levels up to a few hundred billion times the brightness of the Sun, potentially outshining the entire galaxy.

The energy output of AGNs is immense because the black hole continuously powers the gas as it falls toward it, reaching temperatures of millions of degrees before being consumed. This process results in an AGN emitting light far more powerful than the combined light of the galaxy's stars. Despite the small size of black holes, even supermassive ones, they can produce more energy than an entire galaxy of stars. This intense radiation can be hazardous, as x-rays and ultraviolet light can strip away the ozone layer, endangering life on nearby planets.

These objects are often called quasars when they are the most extreme examples of AGNs. The light from such objects can be so bright that it dominates the center of the galaxy, creating a dangerous and awe-inspiring environment. AGNs are an extraordinary phenomenon in astronomy, and their study reveals the powerful and dangerous forces at play in the universe.


The Cosmological Principle is a foundational concept in cosmology, the study of the universe as a whole. It suggests that there is no special part of the universe, implying that the universe is the same everywhere on large scales. This principle is connected to the Copernican Principle, which challenges the idea that Earth holds a unique position in the universe, a debate that once centered around geocentric versus heliocentric models.

The Cosmological Principle has two key components: homogeneity and isotropy. Homogeneity means that, on large scales, one part of the universe is similar to any other part. Isotropy means that no matter which direction you look, the universe appears the same. Initially considered an assumption, these ideas were tested and confirmed starting in the 1980s with surveys of the universe. These findings revealed that, regardless of where we observe, the universe appears uniform and consistent on large scales.

However, the principle only applies to large-scale structures. On smaller scales, such as within our solar system, galaxy, or local group of galaxies, the universe is clearly not homogeneous or isotropic. For example, the inner and outer solar system are distinct, and the Milky Way’s disk, bulge, and halo differ. Similarly, the Local Group of galaxies is sparse compared to the dense Virgo Cluster. Only when we examine scales larger than galaxy clusters, superclusters, or voids—areas so vast that light would take over 300 million years to traverse—does the universe exhibit homogeneity and isotropy.

Once these large scales are reached, the universe shows a remarkable consistency, confirming that every part of the universe is fundamentally the same.


The Hubble-Lemaître Law emerges from the combined observations of two astronomers: Vesto Slipher and Edwin Hubble. Slipher used the Doppler effect to measure the speeds of galaxies, while Hubble measured the distances to galaxies using variable stars. If the motions of galaxies were random, one would expect an equal number of galaxies moving toward us and away from us. However, this was not the case. The majority of galaxies were found to have redshifts, indicating they were moving away from us. While only a few local galaxies were moving toward us, over a million galaxies were observed to be moving away, which was a surprising result.

Hubble combined the motion data with the distance measurements and discovered a pattern: galaxies closer to us were moving away slowly, while more distant galaxies were moving away more quickly. Notably, galaxies that were twice as far away were moving away twice as fast, and galaxies ten times farther away were moving away ten times faster. This linear relationship, expressed as x = y, indicated a highly non-random pattern in the motion of galaxies. This discovery was foundational in understanding the expansion of the universe.

The significance of this finding is amplified by the work of Georges Lemaître, a mathematician who contributed to the formulation of what we now know as the Big Bang theory. Lemaître's work suggested that the universe had a beginning, which led to controversy among some scientists who perceived it as an attempt to introduce religious ideas into science. Despite this, Lemaître’s contributions were crucial to our understanding of the universe's expansion. The broader lesson from Lemaître's experience is that scientific progress thrives on diverse perspectives, highlighting the importance of inclusivity in the scientific community to avoid missing crucial insights.


The motion observed in galaxies cannot be explained by the galaxies themselves, as it would require them to coordinate their movements, which is impossible. Instead, this motion is best explained by the expansion of the universe itself. Georges Lemaître's hypothesis suggested that the universe was expanding, and this concept leads to an important distinction between two ideas: expansion through space versus expansion of space.

Expansion through space is the concept we're familiar with—an object moves through pre-existing space to a different location. In contrast, expansion of space refers to the creation of new space between objects, without any physical movement of those objects themselves. An analogy is made with "Magical Carpet Gnomes," who, when they appear, increase the distance between points by adding space between them. Each individual, though stationary, finds that they are farther apart from others as space itself expands. For instance, as one individual stands at a fixed location, the distance between them and other objects increases due to the expansion of space itself, not because they are physically moving. This results in the illusion that the farther an object is, the faster it is receding, but no actual movement occurs.

The key takeaway here is that the universe’s expansion means that all locations are moving away from each other, not because of motion through space, but because the space itself is expanding. From every observer’s perspective, regardless of their position, everything else appears to be moving away. This leads to the realization that the universe has no center, as there are no edges or boundaries. The universe is infinite in all directions, and thus, it cannot have a center or an "outside."

The Big Bang theory, often misunderstood, did not occur in one specific location but everywhere at once. The universe is continually expanding, and space is being created at every point. The expansion of space itself is an ongoing process, meaning the "Big Bang" is not a single event in time but a continual creation of more space everywhere in the universe.

In essence, the universe is not expanding into anything because there is no "outside" to expand into. This concept leads to the philosophical idea of "mu," meaning that certain questions, such as "what is outside the universe?" cannot be answered because they are based on a flawed assumption—the assumption that the universe has an edge.


The universe is infinitely large, and it was infinitely large from the very beginning. However, as it continues to expand, it becomes even more infinitely large. This notion introduces the concept that there are different sizes of infinities. Consider the series of integers (1, 2, 3, 4, etc.), which can continue indefinitely. There are an infinite number of integers, which we’ll call the "red" numbers. Now consider the series of even numbers (2, 4, 6, 8, etc.). This series is also infinite, or "blue" numbers.

At first glance, both the red and blue sets are infinite, but they can be compared in terms of their relative size. If you divide the number of red numbers by the number of blue numbers, you get a ratio of 2. This implies that there are two red numbers for every blue number. Despite both sets being infinite, they are not the same size, which demonstrates that infinities can come in different sizes.

The universe itself is an example of this concept. When it began, it was infinite in size, and as it expands, it grows even larger—into a larger infinity. The idea of different sizes of infinities is a concept explored in advanced mathematics and physics.

This leads to a shift in perspective, as the universe's infinite nature prompts the consideration of other unseen, invisible components of the cosmos. Specifically, the next discussion introduces the idea of dark matter—something invisible yet fundamental to our understanding of the universe's structure and behavior.


Dark Matter, which I refer to as "glass matter," is an invisible substance that we cannot directly observe. To understand its existence, scientists use a method of double-checking their findings. When measuring the total mass in the universe, one approach is to conduct an inventory, counting everything we can observe—stars, gas clouds, planets, etc. This gives us a number, which we'll call "one." Then, scientists measure the motion of objects in space, which is influenced by gravity. By analyzing these motions, they can determine the amount of mass causing the gravitational forces. This method yields a different result—let's call it "six." The discrepancy between these two numbers, with gravity indicating more mass than the visible inventory, has led to the conclusion that there's something missing from our understanding.

The two possible explanations for this mismatch are that either our inventory is incomplete, meaning we are missing a significant portion of the universe's mass (around 85%), or our understanding of gravity is flawed. After decades of debate, the scientific community now accepts that our understanding of gravity is correct and that we are missing a form of matter. This missing matter does not emit or absorb light, which is why it's called "dark matter," or in my terms, "glass matter." Glass matter is invisible because it doesn't interact with light in the way that visible objects do. Glass, like dark matter, does not emit light, but it also does not absorb it, making it transparent.

Glass matter is hypothesized to be composed of particles similar to neutrons or neutrinos—particles that lack charge and do not interact with the electromagnetic force, which is responsible for light. As a result, glass matter does not glow or block light. This substance is thought to exist as a gas, with particles moving freely, which means it follows the principles of pressure and gravity. Galaxies, therefore, are massive clouds of glass matter, and the galactic halo—the large, "empty" region surrounding a galaxy—is defined by the extent of this invisible material. The matter that forms stars, planets, and other visible objects makes up only a small portion of the galaxy; the majority of the galaxy's mass is in the form of glass matter.

Glass matter also has a temperature, as its particles move at random speeds. However, since it does not interact with light, it doesn't emit or absorb light the way atomic matter does, which affects its temperature. This limits how glass matter's temperature can change. Consequently, the pressure of glass matter and its balance with gravity are influenced by these constraints. By studying this balance, scientists can better understand the role of glass matter in the structure and behavior of galaxies.




There is substantial evidence supporting the existence of glass (dark) matter. One key piece of evidence comes from the study of galaxy rotation curves. According to Kepler's Laws, gravity should decrease with distance, meaning that the orbital speed of stars should decrease as they move farther from the center of a galaxy. However, observations show that the speed of stars at the outer edges of galaxies does not diminish as expected. This suggests the presence of additional, unseen mass throughout the galaxy, including the outer regions beyond the visible stars. This extra mass is attributed to glass matter, which extends beyond the visible edge of the galaxy, forming a halo.

Further evidence comes from galaxy clusters. In these clusters, galaxies orbit at high speeds, but if only atomic matter existed, these galaxies should have enough velocity to escape the cluster's gravity, causing the cluster to disband. However, this does not occur. The presence of an atomic halo gas that gives off x-rays suggests that this gas has high pressure. The gravitational pull from visible atoms is not strong enough to counteract the gas's pressure, which should cause the gas to escape. Since this does not happen, there must be additional mass in the form of glass matter, which provides the necessary gravity to hold the galaxies and the gas within the cluster.

The nature of glass matter has been debated, with two hypotheses competing for explanation. One idea is that glass matter could consist of black holes, forming what is called the MAssive Compact Halo Object (MACHO) hypothesis. The other idea is that it could consist of small particles, such as neutrons or neutrinos, known as the Weakly Interacting Massive Particle (WIMP) hypothesis. To test these ideas, astronomers used gravitational lensing, a phenomenon where gravity bends the path of light. If glass matter were made of black holes, they would bend the light from background stars as they moved through the halo. However, observations made at a telescope in Chile showed almost no gravitational lensing, indicating that glass matter is not composed of black holes. Instead, the results supported the WIMP hypothesis, suggesting that glass matter is a gas spread evenly throughout the galaxy's halo.

This evidence points to the conclusion that glass matter is a gas, consisting of particles like neutrons or neutrinos, which do not interact with light and are spread throughout galaxy halos, contributing to the observed gravitational effects.


The expansion of the Universe has significant implications for its past and present. As the Universe expands, its gas-like contents, including atoms and dark matter, become less dense and cooler. Looking back in time, the Universe was smaller, meaning it was denser and hotter in the past. While this is the average behavior of the Universe, individual parts can have different experiences, such as the core of the sun, which may become hotter as the Universe as a whole cools. This concept forms the core of the Big Bang theory, explaining how the Universe has evolved over time.

The principle of lookback time is essential to understanding cosmic events. Since nothing travels instantaneously, information, including light, takes time to reach us. For example, sunlight takes approximately 8 minutes to travel from the Sun to Earth. If the Sun were to suddenly disappear, we would not notice for another 8 minutes. Similarly, the closest star system, Alpha Centauri, is over four years away by light, meaning that any aliens observing Earth would be seeing information that is over four years old. This time delay becomes even more pronounced when observing distant galaxies, like Andromeda, which is over two million light-years away. Observing such distant objects means that we are seeing them as they were millions of years ago, making this form of astronomical observation a method of cosmic archaeology.

As we look further into the Universe, we are essentially looking back in time. This allows us to observe the Universe at different stages of its history. By comparing galaxies that are nearby with those far away, we can study the changes that galaxies undergo over time, providing valuable insights into the Universe's evolution. However, there is a limit to how far we can observe. This limitation is tied to the finite age of the Universe, explaining Olber's paradox. The paradox is resolved by the fact that light has only been traveling for a finite amount of time, meaning that the Universe has an age, and there is a limit to how far back we can look.


The concept of the age of the Universe is rooted in the idea that the further we look into space, the further back in time we are observing. As we continue looking farther away, we reach a point where we can no longer look any further, signaling a boundary to our observational reach. This is because, according to the Big Bang theory, as we go back in time, the Universe was smaller, denser, and hotter. The further we trace back, the smaller, denser, and hotter it becomes, eventually reaching a point where all matter and energy were concentrated in a single place, with infinite density and temperature. This moment marks the beginning of the Universe, which is referred to as the Big Bang.

Determining when the Big Bang occurred is relatively straightforward. Just as one can estimate how long a car has been driving based on its speed and distance, we can calculate the age of the Universe by observing the speed at which galaxies are moving away from us. By knowing the distance of galaxies and their rate of recession, we can estimate how long they have been expanding. This is similar to the way we calculate the speed of an object moving away from us. However, this equation only works under the assumption that the Universe has been expanding at a constant rate, which is not entirely the case.

Gravity, in fact, is acting against the expansion of the Universe. Just as gravity slows down a thrown object, it works to slow the Universe's expansion. Gravity tends to pull everything together, meaning that the expansion of the Universe is being gradually slowed down. To understand how much the Universe has slowed over time, we need to consider the amount of gravity present in the Universe. This requires applying the insights of Albert Einstein, whose work on gravity and spacetime has become essential in understanding how it affects the expansion of the Universe.


Isaac Newton's view of gravity as a force is incomplete. In reality, gravity is better understood as a curvature of space itself, a concept introduced by Albert Einstein. While Newton and Einstein both agreed that objects move in straight lines unless acted upon by a force, Einstein took this a step further by considering what happens when space itself is curved.

In a flat, level space, an object can move in a straight line at a constant speed. However, if space is curved, like in the case of Earth orbiting the Sun, the object's path will curve as well. The Sun curves space around it, and Earth moves forward, but due to the curvature of space, it follows a curved path, resulting in an orbit. If space is curved enough, an object could continue to orbit indefinitely, eventually returning to its starting point.

This understanding of gravity, as a curvature of space, leads to a new perspective on how gravity works in the Universe, further refining our understanding of cosmic phenomena like the expansion of the Universe.


The age of the Universe can be determined by understanding the amount of gravity in the Universe and how it has slowed the expansion over time. This calculation allows us to adjust for any potential factors, such as pressure from dark energy, that could have influenced the expansion rate. After correcting for these variables, we arrive at an estimate of the Universe’s age: approximately 13.799 billion years. The uncertainty range is narrow, between 13.778 billion and 13.820 billion years, a remarkably small margin of error—equivalent to knowing someone's age not just in years, but in months.

To put this into context, the solar system is about 4.567 billion years old, meaning it has existed for roughly the last third of the Universe's history. The first two-thirds of the Universe's existence occurred without the solar system or Earth. Additionally, while we have yet to find anything older than 13.799 billion years, the oldest stars we know of are about 12 billion years old, consistent with the Big Bang theory.

Another line of evidence supporting the Big Bang theory is the presence of helium in the Universe, which further confirms our understanding of cosmic history.


Early in the Universe's history, fusion played a key role in the formation of elements. Modern stars have more Helium and heavier elements due to previous generations of stars, where fusion events in supernovae created heavier elements like Carbon, Oxygen, and Iron. In contrast, early stars, formed in the early Universe, had little to no heavy elements. However, they were still composed of about 25% Helium, which raised questions about its origin.

Helium in these early stars suggests that fusion had already occurred. Even gas clouds outside galaxies, which have never formed stars, contain Helium. This raised the question: did fusion occur everywhere in the Universe, implying conditions like those in a star—hot and dense? The answer lies in the early history of the Universe, when it was much smaller, denser, and hotter. In the early moments after the Big Bang, the entire Universe was in a state where fusion was possible, similar to the core of a star.

As the Universe expanded, it cooled. The speed of expansion directly impacted the rate of cooling. A fast expansion would result in rapid cooling, leading to only a brief period of fusion, producing limited Helium. A slower expansion would allow for more prolonged fusion, resulting in more Helium. This relationship between expansion speed and Helium production has been tested and found to match observations, supporting the Big Bang theory.

Additionally, fusion not only produced Helium but also heavier elements. However, the Universe's cooling meant that while there was sufficient fuel for fusion, the temperature was too low for the creation of heavy elements. Only in a cooling environment could the early stages of fusion occur, but not the later stages required to produce heavy elements. This observation aligns perfectly with the predictions of the Big Bang theory.

The Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation is another key piece of evidence supporting the Big Bang.


The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation is a crucial piece of evidence for the Big Bang theory. After the period of fusion, the Universe cooled enough for neutral atoms to form. In the early Universe, atoms were ionized due to the high temperatures, meaning electrons were knocked out of atoms by high-energy light. This prevented light from traveling freely, as it kept interacting with the charged particles.

However, as the Universe cooled, atoms became neutral, and the light no longer interacted with the electrons and protons as before. This allowed the light to travel freely across the Universe. The light from this time, the radiation from the Big Bang, has a distinct spectrum that can be observed today. This specific spectrum provides direct evidence for the Big Bang, as it matches the expected radiation produced during that time.

The spectrum of the CMB provides critical support for the Big Bang theory by showing the expected characteristics of the radiation that filled the Universe after atoms became neutral. This radiation is a remnant from the early Universe, and its properties confirm the conditions that the Universe must have experienced at that time.


Around 300,000 years after the Big Bang, the Universe reached a stage where the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation was formed. For a time, the Universe continued to expand and cool without significant events occurring, or so it seemed. Before neutral atoms could form, light was constantly bouncing off protons and electrons, exerting pressure on them, which prevented atoms from collapsing under gravity. However, dark matter, unlike regular matter, was not affected by this light pressure.

Dark matter began to gather together through gravity, forming halos. These halos, which collected dark matter gas, acted as the seeds for the formation of galaxies. Once atoms became neutral and no longer interacted with the light, they were able to be pulled into these dark matter halos. It’s important to note that dark matter is about five times more abundant than atomic matter, meaning that galaxies are, in essence, halos of dark matter gas. This view challenges the bias of thinking that atomic matter, which makes up humans and planets, is the central element in a galaxy.

As dark matter halos continued to grow heavier under gravity, they also began to merge with one another. These mergers led to the formation of increasingly massive galaxies. Over time, smaller halos (and thus smaller galaxies) collided to form medium-sized halos, which merged into even larger halos, eventually forming galaxy clusters. This process of hierarchical structure formation can be likened to smaller objects combining to form larger ones, similar to how dust, rocks, and asteroids can form planets. Simulations of this process show how galaxies continue to grow and form clusters, leaving behind voids in the process.


As dark matter halos continue to evolve, the atomic gas within them cools and undergoes gravitational contraction, forming a dense cloud at the center of the halo. However, unlike later stages of star formation, this first cloud of gas, made mostly of hydrogen and helium, does not contain heavy elements and therefore cannot cool as quickly. Consequently, this cloud does not fragment into multiple smaller clumps of gas that could form stars. Instead, the entire cloud contracts as a single entity, forming one supermassive star, far larger than typical stars like O-type stars. These supermassive stars are extraordinarily luminous, hot, and short-lived. Upon their death, they undergo a massive supernova explosion, dispersing heavy elements throughout the halo and even into intergalactic space.

The first supernova explosions enrich the halo with heavy elements, allowing subsequent generations of stars to form in a process similar to what we understand about star formation. These early stars, called globular clusters, form within the gas-rich environment of the halo. Meanwhile, the core of the supermassive star becomes the seed for a supermassive black hole. When such a massive star dies, it leaves behind a supermassive black hole, which continues to grow by accreting nearby gas, marking the beginning of the quasar era. As atomic gas clouds orbit around this black hole, stars continue to form, contributing to the formation of what we recognize as a galaxy: a supermassive black hole at the center with stars and gas clouds orbiting within a dark matter halo.

This process is still largely theoretical, and much of the understanding presented here is based on current hypotheses. The James Webb Space Telescope, however, is poised to provide more solid evidence by observing the earliest galaxies formed in the Universe. Test images from the telescope show distant galaxies, and astronomers are eagerly awaiting its official data to confirm these early galactic formation theories.


The fate of the Universe depends on the balance between its expansion and the gravitational pull of its matter. The concept of escape velocity, which describes the speed needed to overcome Earth's gravity, can be applied to determine whether the Universe will continue expanding or eventually collapse. If the expansion of the Universe is weaker than the force of gravity, it will slow down, stop, and then reverse, causing everything to contract back together in a "Big Crunch." This would result in a smaller, denser, and hotter Universe, essentially reversing the Big Bang.

On the other hand, if the expansion of the Universe is stronger than its gravitational pull, the Universe will continue to expand indefinitely. Gravity will slow the expansion, but it won't stop it, leading to a state where the Universe becomes increasingly larger, more diffuse, and cooler. This outcome is called "The Big Chill."

To determine the actual fate of the Universe, the critical factor is the ratio of gravitational force to expansion. If the amount of matter in the Universe is greater than a specific threshold (more than "1"), gravity will eventually overcome expansion, leading to a Big Crunch. If the amount of matter is less than this threshold (less than "1"), the Universe will continue expanding forever.


In 1998, astronomers discovered that the Universe is expanding at an accelerating rate, rather than slowing down as previously expected. This finding contradicted earlier predictions that the Universe's expansion would eventually be stopped by gravity. After measuring the mass of the Universe, astronomers found that atoms and dark matter combined only accounted for 29% of the required matter to halt the expansion, leading to the conclusion that the Universe was heading toward a Big Chill. However, the unexpected discovery revealed that the Universe is actually speeding up, not slowing down, challenging the previous understanding of gravity and expansion.

The cause of this acceleration was traced to a mysterious form of pressure, which astronomers named Dark Energy. Dark energy makes up approximately 71% of the Universe's composition, and its exact nature remains unknown. While dark energy's effects are clear, its origin, how it works, and what its future holds are still unresolved questions in astrophysics. The influence of dark energy is driving the expansion of the Universe at an increasing rate, meaning galaxies will continue to move farther apart, with the Milky Way eventually merging with the Andromeda galaxy before this acceleration reaches extreme levels.

This discovery has revolutionized our understanding of the Universe's fate, suggesting that a Big Chill might not be the final outcome, but rather an ongoing acceleration toward ever-expanding space. The future remains uncertain, with dark energy playing a central role in shaping the Universe's destiny.


The Universe is currently in the Era of Stars, a time when atomic gas is converted into stars that form, die, and recycle material to create subsequent generations. This process extends the lifespan of stars, but eventually, the available gas will be exhausted. Over time, stars will cease to form. The sequence of stellar deaths will proceed from the massive O and B stars, which end their lives in supernovae, to progressively smaller stars. The last to perish will be the long-lived M stars, which barely perform fusion. This era will last approximately 100 trillion years, about 10,000 times the current age of the Universe.

Following the Era of Stars is the Era of Degeneracy. In this phase, the only luminous objects will be white dwarfs and neutron stars, emitting residual heat as they gradually cool. Eventually, these remnants will fade to darkness. Over vast timescales, even matter itself will disintegrate, as protons and neutrons undergo radioactive decay into photons and antimatter electrons. By this point, the Universe will consist of dark matter clouds and supermassive black holes. This era will extend to roughly 1 0 40 10 40 years.

The Era of Black Holes begins once all normal matter has decayed. Black holes, although seemingly eternal, are not completely black. Through Hawking Radiation, a quantum process, black holes gradually lose mass as particles quantum-tunnel out of them. However, this process is extraordinarily slow. The evaporation of the last supermassive black hole is estimated to occur around 1 0 100 10 100 years, a timespan far beyond human comprehension.

After the final black hole evaporates, the future of the Universe becomes entirely speculative. The absence of significant structures or processes renders predictions impossible. What lies beyond the Era of Black Holes remains unknown.


Life is fundamentally a complex system, relying on complex chemicals interacting in intricate ways. To explore the conditions necessary for life, we focus on two critical components: the chemistry of life and the environment in which it can occur.

Carbon Chemistry Carbon is unique in its ability to form an incredible variety of chemical structures, including chains, rings, spheres, and self-replicating molecules. These properties make carbon the easiest element to build life from. The periodic table shows that silicon, directly below carbon, has similar chemical properties, suggesting the possibility of silicon-based lifeforms. While silicon chemistry is less versatile than carbon’s, its complexity, demonstrated by the chemistry of rocks, cannot be ignored as a potential basis for life elsewhere in the Universe.

Liquids as a Medium Life also requires a medium where chemical interactions can occur. Solids restrict interactions to neighboring molecules, and gases allow interactions but lack the stability needed for meaningful chemical reactions. Liquids provide an ideal middle ground, facilitating chemical movement and interaction while maintaining stability. Among known liquids, water is unparalleled in its suitability for supporting life.

The Role of Water Water’s unique properties stem from its molecular imbalance. The oxygen in a water molecule has a partial negative charge, while the hydrogens have a partial positive charge, creating a dipole. This leads to hydrogen bonding, where water molecules attract each other, making water "sticky." This stickiness gives water an unusually wide temperature range in which it remains a liquid, from 0°C to 100°C. It also makes water an excellent solvent for charged substances, including acids, bases, and salts, enabling vital biochemical reactions.

By comparison, methane, another liquid found in the Universe, lacks water's advantages. Methane is molecularly balanced, making it poor at dissolving other chemicals. Its liquid range is also narrow, spanning only about 20°C, making it a less stable medium for life.

Conclusion The most plausible form of life is carbon-based, utilizing water as its medium. This combination provides the necessary complexity, stability, and chemical versatility to support life processes. While alternative chemistries and solvents are theoretically possible, carbon in water remains the easiest and most familiar path to life.


Exomoons, or moons orbiting planets in other solar systems, present intriguing possibilities for habitability. One mechanism for sustaining life on these moons is tidal heating. Similar to the conditions on Europa, Enceladus, and Titan in our solar system, tidal forces from a massive host planet can generate enough internal heat to maintain subsurface liquid water. This heating mechanism could create environments where life might thrive.

Another possibility involves planetary migration. Gas giants significantly larger than Jupiter, with proportionally larger moons, may migrate closer to their star and enter the habitable zone of their solar system. While gas giants themselves lack a solid surface to support life, their massive moons could become habitable if they are large enough to retain atmospheres and liquid water. Jupiter's moon Ganymede, which is larger than Mercury, demonstrates that such massive moons are feasible. If similar moons orbit gas giants in the habitable zones of other systems, they could support Earth-like conditions.

The discovery of many "hot Jupiters"—gas giants that have migrated close to their stars—supports the plausibility of habitable exomoons. These systems offer numerous opportunities for life to exist outside of traditional Earth-like planets, broadening the scope of astrobiological exploration.
